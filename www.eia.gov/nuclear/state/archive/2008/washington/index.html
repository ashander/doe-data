Washington Nuclear Profile                                     
2008 Profiles

State Overview

There is one operating nuclear power plant in Washington:

    Columbia Generating Station in Benton County
  • Five commercial reactors were initially planned for the State by the Washington Public Power System, but Units 4 and 5 were cancelled in 1982. Units 1 and 3 were cancelled in 1995.
  • In 2000, Washington Public Power System changed its name to Energy Northwest and the plant's name from WNP-2 to the Columbia Generating Station.
  • Since the retirement of Oregon's Trojan Nuclear Plant, it is the only licensed commercial reactor in the northwestern United States.

Contribution of Nuclear Power

Among States with nuclear power, Washington has the fourth smallest nuclear capacity.

Columbia Generating Station represents about 4% of the Washington’s total electric generating capacity, placing nuclear power fifth behind hydroelectric, natural gas, coal and wind.

Columbia Generating Station accounts for nearly 8% of Washington’s total generation.

Washington is a net exporter of electricity, exporting 12% more electric energy than it used.

License Renewals & New Applications

  • Columbia: On January 20, 2010, Energy Northwest submitted a license renewal application to the NRC for the Columbia Generating Station. The current operating license will expire in December 2023.

Operator: Energy Northwest

Location and Service Territory: This site covers 1,089 acres of Benton County, on the Hanford site of the Department of Energy.

Construction Cost: $6.392 billion (2007 USD)

Reactor Description: The Columbia Generating Station unit is a General Electric Type 5 boiling water reactor.

Cooling System: The plant is cooled by mechanical draft towers supplied by water from the Columbia River.
