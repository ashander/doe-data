Nebraska State Energy Profile



Nebraska Quick Facts

  • Nebraska ranked second in the nation, after Iowa, in corn-based ethanol production capacity in 2015. 
  • Nebraska obtains about three-fifths of its net electricity generation from coal and about one-fourth from nuclear power.
  • The National Renewable Energy Laboratory estimates that more than 90% of Nebraska has suitable conditions for commercial-scale wind-powered electricity generation. 
  • Nebraska is among the top 10 states in per capita energy consumption because of its energy-intensive industrial sector, led by food processing and chemical manufacturing.
  • Farm irrigation is electricity-intensive and seasonal; it entails high costs for electricity that increase the average reported cost of electricity for Nebraska’s industrial sector.
  • In 2015, Nebraska obtained more than 10% of its total net electricity generation from renewables, and wind supplied almost three-fourths of the renewable generation.

Analysis

Last Updated: February 18, 2016

Overview

Located in the center of the continental United States, Nebraska's plains have fertile soils and considerable renewable resources but few fossil energy resources.1,2 Nebraska's climate varies greatly from season to season, with hot summers and frigid winters. The state's highest recorded temperature is 118 degrees Fahrenheit and the lowest, minus 47 degrees.3 Tornadoes and thunderstorms are common in the spring and summer. The state is located in the nation's Tornado Alley, where warm air from the south often meets polar air from the north. Although the western half of the state is semiarid, Nebraska is a leading agricultural state. Corn is the most important crop, and the state is one of the nation's leading producers of ethanol.4,5 Nebraska's wide plains provide some of the nation's best wind energy potential.6 Hydropower and biomass resources are also used for electricity generation in the state.7,8

Nebraska is one of the world's major meatpacking centers. Its energy-intensive food processing industry leads the manufacturing sector.

Nebraska ranks among the 10 highest states in energy consumption per person.9 The industrial sector is the largest end-use energy consumer in the state.10 Nebraska is one of the world's major meatpacking centers, and the energy-intensive food processing industry leads the state's manufacturing sector. Chemical manufacturing—particularly of pharmaceuticals, pesticides, and fertilizers—and machinery manufacturing are also major energy-intensive industries in the state.11,12 Farm irrigation is also electricity-intensive, as well as seasonal; it entails high costs for electricity that increase the average reported cost of electricity for Nebraska's industrial sector.13 Transportation, Nebraska's second-largest end-use energy-consuming sector, uses only about half as much energy as the industrial sector.14 With the state's harsh winters and hot summers, per capita energy consumption by Nebraska's residential sector is among the largest in the nation.15 Almost two-thirds of Nebraska households heat their homes with natural gas, another one-fourth use electricity, and about 8% use liquefied petroleum gas (LPG).16

Petroleum

Nebraska began producing crude oil commercially in 1939. Annual production peaked at nearly 25 million barrels in 1962.17 In 2014, Nebraska's crude oil production was about one-eighth of its peak.18 The Niobrara shale formation, which is an emerging oil play in Colorado and Wyoming, extends into Nebraska, but significant Niobrara crude oil production has not been established in the state.19 Nebraska does not have any crude oil refining capacity.20 A network of petroleum product pipelines connects Nebraska markets to refining centers in nearby states.21 Crude oil moves via pipeline through southern Nebraska on its way from Wyoming to refineries in the Midwest.22 A proposed additional crude oil pipeline, routed through the Sand Hills of north-central and northwestern Nebraska, has stalled.23 An area of sloping hills and small lakes, the Sand Hills region sits on top of the Ogallala Aquifer.24 The Ogallala, a vast, shallow aquifer that extends beneath parts of eight states, provides groundwater crucial for agriculture in the semiarid Plains states, particularly Nebraska,25,26 and is considered environmentally sensitive. 27

Nebraska has no U.S. Environmental Protection Agency-identified non-attainment areas for air quality and allows the statewide use of conventional motor gasoline without ethanol added.28,29 However, Nebraska is the nation's second-largest producer of ethanol30 and has more than 70 fueling stations selling E85, a blend of 85% ethanol and 15% gasoline.31,32

Natural gas

Natural gas production in Nebraska has been declining. With less than 1 billion cubic feet of natural gas marketed in 2014 from more than 300 producing wells statewide, Nebraska must rely on interstate deliveries to meet most of its natural gas needs.33,34,35 Natural gas produced in the Rocky Mountain region, Texas, and Oklahoma enters Nebraska by pipeline from Wyoming, Kansas, and Colorado.36 More than nine-tenths of the natural gas entering Nebraska is shipped on to other states.37 Some of the natural gas received in Nebraska is stored in the state's one storage field, which has a total capacity of about 35 billion cubic feet.38

Nebraska's leading natural gas-consuming sector is industry.39 The residential sector, the second-highest natural gas-consuming sector, uses less than half as much as the industrial sector, even though nearly two-thirds of Nebraska households use natural gas as their primary fuel for home heating.40 The commercial sector uses less natural gas than the residential sector, and less than 5% of natural gas delivered to end-users in Nebraska is used for electric power generation.

Coal

Nebraska has no commercial coal production and must acquire all the coal it needs from out of state.41 Nearly all of the coal consumed in Nebraska arrives by rail from Wyoming.42 Most of the coal is used for electricity generation. A very small amount of coal is delivered to industrial plants as well.43

Electricity

Coal-fired power plants typically supply about three-fifths of Nebraska's net electricity generation, and nuclear power usually contributes about one-fourth.44,45 Nebraska's two nuclear power plants, Fort Calhoun and Cooper, are located along the Missouri River on the state's eastern border.46,47 In 2011, several factors, including Missouri River flooding, led to Fort Calhoun's being taken offline for more than two years for a series of upgrades.48 The state's coal-fired power plants made up most of the shortfall in net electricity generation during that period.49 Nebraska gets most of the rest of its net electricity generation from wind, plus small amounts of conventional hydroelectric power, natural gas, biomass, and petroleum. 50

Nebraska has one uranium mine, and ore from the mine helps fuel one of the state's two nuclear power plants.51 Uranium was discovered in Nebraska in 1980, and operations began at the Crow Butte mine in the state's northwestern corner in 1991.52 As of 2014, Nebraska had one of only six operating uranium in-situ-leach plants in the nation.53

Nebraska obtains the majority of its renewable electricity generation from wind power.

Nebraska typically produces a little more electricity than it consumes.54 Electricity consumption in the state is fairly evenly divided among the industrial, commercial, and residential sectors.55 Slightly more than one-fourth of Nebraska households rely on electricity for their primary home heating needs.56 Nebraska's retail electricity rates are in the lowest third of state rates.57 Nebraska is the only state in which all electricity providers are non-profit organizations—either public power districts, municipal power systems, or rural electric cooperatives.58

Renewable energy

Renewable resources fuel about one-tenth of Nebraska's electricity generation,59 and the state has substantial undeveloped potential. Wind is the largest source of Nebraska's renewable electricity generation. In 2015, Nebraska obtained about three-fourths of its renewable generation from wind energy, and the share from wind has been increasing in recent years.60,61 About nine-tenths of the state is estimated to have suitable conditions for utility-scale wind-powered electricity generation, and Nebraska is fifth among the states in available land area suitable for wind power.62,63

Hydroelectric facilities produce most of the rest of Nebraska's renewable electricity generation. Nebraska typically obtains less than 5% of its total net electricity generation from hydroelectric power, with annual generation varying with water availability.64 Fewer than a dozen of the state's 2,800 dams have hydroelectric generating facilities.65,66,67 Most dams are earthen embankments,68 and there is little potential for further utility-scale hydroelectric facilities, although micro-hydroelectric generators that use natural water flows may be feasible.69 Nebraska also has substantial solar power potential,70,71 but solar development has not been economical in most regions.72 Small areas in the northwest and along the north-central border of the state have moderate geothermal energy potential.73,74

Nebraska is the nation's second largest producer of ethanol.

Nebraska is a national leader in biofuels. The state is second only to Iowa in the production of corn-based ethanol.75 Nebraska has 25 active ethanol-producing plants spread across the state, with the highest concentrations in southern Nebraska. Producers in the state use more than 700 million bushels of grain to manufacture almost 1.8 billion gallons of ethanol per year.76 Most of the ethanol produced in Nebraska is shipped to other states.77 Nebraska also has two commercial biodiesel plants, but both have suspended production. A third facility, built in 2008 but never operational, is being retrofitted to use a variety of feedstocks, including waste oils, with startup planned during 2016.78,79 Nebraska also has biomass potential from woody biomass, crop residues, manure management, and municipal solid waste.80

Nebraska does not have a renewable energy standard.80 The state does have a number of renewable energy tax credits, as well as interconnection and net metering rules for solar photovoltaics, landfill gas, wind, biomass, hydroelectric, geothermal electric, anaerobic digestion, and small hydroelectric power generation.82 Nebraska also has a statewide building energy code.83
