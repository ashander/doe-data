Profile AnalysisPrint State Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: February 18, 2016

Overview

Kansas stretches more than 400 miles from east to west. Its broad plains rise gradually from about 600 feet above sea level at the Missouri River on the east to more than 3,000 feet higher on the western High Plains.1 Many rivers flow from the High Plains eastward across Kansas. Major river systems include the Arkansas River, which flows to the Mississippi River, and the Kansas, Republican, and Smoky Hill Rivers, which are tributaries to the Missouri River.2 Kansas has hot summers and often-frigid winters. Severe weather, including tornados, is not uncommon in Kansas when warm moist air from the Gulf of Mexico meets cold polar air from the north.3

Kansas has both considerable energy resources and considerable energy potential. Substantial crude oil and natural gas reserves are found in several basins across the state,4 including part of one of the nation's largest natural gas fields.5 With its open prairie, Kansas has strong winds that provide significant wind energy resources.6 An important agricultural state,7 Kansas ranks third in the nation in the amount of land devoted to farms.8 The state's grain sorghum and corn crops are major feedstocks for ethanol production.9 Most of the biomass potential in Kansas comes from municipal solid waste, landfill gas, and agricultural waste.10 Kansas has one operating hydroelectric plant, and the potential exists for development of additional hydroelectric resources on its rivers.11,12 The state averages more than 200 days of full or partial sunshine each year,13 giving Kansas significant solar energy resources.14,15

Total end-use energy consumption in Kansas is highest in the industrial sector,16 which includes manufacturing, particularly aviation and aerospace manufacturing, as well as agriculture and the energy-intensive petroleum industry.17,18 Transportation, the second-largest energy-consuming sector, uses about two-thirds as much energy as the industrial sector.19

Petroleum

Oil fields span Kansas in a broad arc that includes all but a few counties in the north central portion of the state.20 Kansas produces about 1.5% of total U.S. crude oil, and the state is one of the nation's top 10 crude oil-producing states.21 An 1892 crude oil discovery near Neodesha, Kansas, is considered the first significant oil well west of the Mississippi River and was the first to indicate the vast oil potential of the Mid-Continent region.22

Kansas is the source of about 1.5% of total U.S. crude oil production.

Kansas is also an oil-refining state. A network of pipelines delivers crude oil to the state's three refineries, which have a combined capacity of about 340,000 barrels of crude oil per calendar day.23 The refineries produce a variety of refined products, including diesel fuel and motor gasoline.24 Kansas is one of the top 10 states in per capita consumption of all petroleum products and among the leading 5 states in per capita consumption of liquefied petroleum gas (LPG).25,26 LPG is used to dry corn at harvest,27 and Kansas is among the 10 largest corn-producing states.28 Kansas produces a substantial amount of ethanol from corn at nearly a dozen production plants,29 and E-85 ethanol fuel is available around the state.30 Kansas has only a small area in the eastern part of the state, in the Kansas City metropolitan area, where motor gasoline must be formulated to reduce emissions that contribute to ground-level ozone.31

Natural gas

Kansas produces about 1% of the nation's natural gas.32 The first natural gas was found in 1882, and cumulative production from all Kansas natural gas wells through 2012 exceeded 42.3 trillion cubic feet.33 The Hugoton Field, one of many natural gas and oil fields in the 12,000-square-mile Hugoton Gas Area, is one of the largest natural gas fields in North America and covers much of southwestern Kansas.34,35 Natural gas was discovered in the Kansas portion of the Hugoton Field in 1922 by drillers seeking crude oil. Natural gas was of little interest until 1929 when the first pipeline to deliver natural gas to nearby markets was built.36 The state's natural gas marketed production has declined overall since 1995.37 Coalbed methane production from coal seams in the eastern part of the state38 has offset some of the decline.39 At the end of 2014, natural gas reserves in coal seams were estimated at more than 210 billion cubic feet.40 Total Kansas natural gas reserves were estimated at 4.6 trillion cubic feet.41

The Hugoton Gas Field in Kansas is one of the largest natural gas fields in the nation.

In addition to natural gas and crude oil, the Hugoton Field contains unusually high concentrations of helium and has the largest reserves of helium in the United States. The helium is separated out of natural gas and is piped to the National Helium Reserve in Amarillo, Texas, where it is stored in underground geologic formations.42

Thirteen interstate natural gas pipelines cross Kansas.43 Natural gas enters the state via pipelines from Oklahoma, Nebraska, and Colorado,44 and pipelines ship natural gas out of state, primarily to Missouri and Nebraska.45 Kansas consumes most of the natural gas it produces.46,47 The Mid-Continent Center, a 194-mile pipeline system in south-central Kansas, is a key natural gas interconnect, merging production from several states in the region and piping it east toward major natural gas-consuming markets.48 Kansas also has 17 natural gas storage fields49 with a combined capacity in excess of 280 billion cubic feet.50

The industrial sector in Kansas consumes almost half of the natural gas used in the state, more than any other sector.51 Although two-thirds of Kansas households use natural gas as their primary energy source for home heating,52 the residential sector accounts for only slightly more than one-fourth of the state's natural gas consumption.53

Coal

Kansas coal mining began in the 1850s with shallow mines in the eastern part of the state. Significant amounts of coal were produced from many mines in Kansas in the late 19th century, primarily to power railroad locomotives.54 Currently, the only active coal mine in Kansas produces small amounts of bituminous coal55,56 that are shipped to industrial users57 or blended with coal from Wyoming at the La Cygne Generating Station,58 the state's second largest electricity generating plant.59 Coal is the largest single fuel source for electricity generation in Kansas,60 and nearly all of the coal used in the state's many coal-fired power plants is low-sulfur subbituminous coal shipped by rail from Wyoming. Minor amounts of coal come from Missouri and Oklahoma. 61

Electricity

Coal-fired power plants supply almost three-fifths of the net electricity generated in Kansas.62 Coal's share of net generation has been gradually declining as wind energy's share has increased;63 in 2014, wind was the second largest source of net generation, producing more than one-fifth of the electricity generated in Kansas.64 The state's single nuclear power station was the third largest generator, supplying about one-sixth of net electricity produced Kansas.65 The state's electricity prices are slightly below the national average,66 in part because of the proximity of Kansas to the coalfields of Wyoming,67 which provide almost all of the coal used for generation.68 Although the state has abundant natural gas reserves, natural gas-fired power plants typically contribute less than 5% of the state's total net electricity generation.69 Fewer than one-fourth of Kansas households rely on electricity as their primary energy source for home heating.70

Renewable energy

Kansas is among the leading states both in wind energy generation and in wind energy potential. Almost all of Kansas's renewable net electricity generation comes from wind,71 and, in 2014, the state ranked among the top five states in the nation in generation from wind energy.72 Kansas is also among the five states with the highest wind energy potential.73,74 In addition, Kansas has solar and hydroelectric power resources. Kansas is among the 10 sunniest states in the country.75 Western Kansas has areas that receive as much sun as parts of the southwestern United States,76,77 but the state's solar potential has yet to be substantially developed.78 Although Kansas is crossed by several major rivers, the state has only one hydroelectric generating facility, which is located on the Kaw River.79

In 2014, wind energy provided more than one-fifth of net electricity generation in Kansas.

Kansas is one of the top 10 ethanol-producing states.80 Corn and grain sorghum are the primary grain sources for the ethanol produced in Kansas each year. There are 14 ethanol plants in the state with a combined capacity of more than 500 million gallons.81 A cellulosic ethanol plant that uses corn stalks, leaves, husks, and cobs, as well as wheat straw, milo stubble, and switchgrass, began operations in 2014.82

In 2015, the Kansas legislature converted the state's renewable portfolio standard (RPS), enacted in May 2009, into a voluntary goal for the state's investor-owned and cooperative electric utilities. The RPS originally required electricity providers to obtain 10% of their peak demand capacity from eligible renewable resources from 2011 through 2015, 15% from 2016 through 2019, and 20% each year from 2020 onward.83 The Kansas RPS is based on generating capacity rather than retail electric sales. Technologies that meet the goal include wind, solar thermal and photovoltaic, generation fueled by crops grown for energy production and some agricultural wastes, and hydroelectric facilities of less than 10 megawatts. Additional legislation has established net metering for customers of investor-owned utilities. In 2014, Kansas legislators reduced the sizes of distributed facilities that are eligible for net metering. Grid-connected distributed facilities may be counted by electricity providers in meeting the providers' RPS goal.84
