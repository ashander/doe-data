Profile AnalysisPrint State Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: March 17, 2016

Overview

Missouri is located near the geographic center of the Lower 48 states at the junction of the nation's two largest rivers, the Mississippi and the Missouri.1 The state's infrastructure and location give shippers the ability to move raw materials and finished products quickly and economically by rail, river, and truck across the state and the country, making Missouri a transportation hub for the nation.2 The state has little fossil fuel production, but it does have fossil fuel resources that have not been fully developed, including tar sands, coalbed methane, and oil shales.3 The rich soils of the plains and rolling hills north of the Missouri River and those of the southeastern lowlands form the state's fertile agricultural regions.4,5 Missouri's corn and soybean crops are feedstocks for the state's biofuels industry.6,7 South of the Missouri River, the heavily forested Ozark Plateau has abundant biomass resource potential, and the open prairies of northern and western Missouri have the state's best wind resource.8,9 Three of the largest earthquakes in U.S. history were centered in southeastern Missouri, and the potential for further tremors in that geologically active part of the state is taken into consideration in siting nuclear power plants throughout the Midwest.10,11

Missouri, near the geographic center of the Lower 48 states, is a national transportation hub.

Missouri has a moderate climate and, despite cold winters and hot summers, extended periods of very cold or very hot weather are uncommon.12 The state's energy consumption per capita is near the national average.13 The transportation sector is the largest energy-consuming end-use sector in Missouri, followed closely by the residential sector, where more than half of the households heat with natural gas.14,15 The industrial sector, which includes agriculture and the manufacture of food products, chemicals, and transportation equipment, accounts for one-fifth of the state's total energy consumption.16,17

Petroleum

Crude oil production in Missouri is modest, less than 0.01% of the nation's total.18 The first petroleum production in the state came from wells drilled near Kansas City, Missouri, in the 1860s, just after the Civil War. Today, crude oil is produced in northwestern Missouri near the state's border with Kansas and Nebraska, and in eastern Missouri near St. Louis.19 Annual crude oil production in the state declined overall from its peak of nearly 300,000 barrels in 1984 to 80,000 barrels in 2007. Production has increased since then, and, in 2013 and 2014, production reached almost 200,000 barrels each year.20 Additional undeveloped petroleum resources exist in the tar sands of western Missouri, and the oil shales in the northern and the extreme southwestern parts of the state.21

The Mississippi and Missouri Rivers provide important transportation routes for petroleum products.

The only petroleum refinery in Missouri closed in 1982, but major crude oil pipelines pass through Missouri on their way to refining centers elsewhere in the Midwest.22,23 Missouri receives finished petroleum products from several pipelines that originate in the Gulf Coast.24 The Mississippi and Missouri Rivers are important transportation routes and some petroleum products also arrive at Missouri's inland ports via river barge. 25,26

Missouri's total petroleum consumption per person is slightly below the national average.27,28 Few Missouri residents use heating oil for home heating, much less than the national average.29,30 However, more liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) is consumed in Missouri than in several more populous states, in part because Missouri is one of the top 10 corn-producing states in the nation and LPG is used to dry corn after harvest.31,32,33,34 The majority of Missouri's petroleum consumption is in the form of motor gasoline.35 Missouri is one of only two Midwestern states that requires statewide use of oxygenated motor gasoline. The St. Louis and Kansas City metropolitan areas have stricter requirements for motor gasoline blends limiting emissions than in the rest of the state.36

Natural gas

Missouri does not have any natural gas reserves and has only a token amount of natural gas production.37 Small amounts of natural gas were produced in Missouri in the past, but, with the exception of a few wells, most of which produce natural gas for private use, production in the state has ceased.38 One-third of Missouri, about 24,000 square miles, is underlain by coal seams that potentially could produce coalbed methane. Those coal deposits are in northwest, north-central, and west-central Missouri.39,40

Missouri is crossed by a dozen interstate natural gas pipelines.41 Natural gas enters the state from the west and south, mostly from Kansas, Arkansas, and Nebraska. Most of the natural gas is sent on to Illinois and Iowa for delivery to markets in the Midwest and beyond.42 The Rockies Express Pipeline (REX) serves the state and is one of the largest natural gas pipelines in the nation. The REX pipeline's western section originates in Colorado and brings Rocky Mountain natural gas east. The pipeline passes near Kansas City, Missouri, before interconnecting in northeastern Missouri with the REX pipeline's eastern section, which continues on to Ohio. The eastern section of the REX pipeline is bidirectional and can bring natural gas to Missouri from the Appalachians as well.43,44 Missouri has one natural gas storage field with a capacity of almost 14 billion cubic feet.45,46 It is located near St. Louis.47

The residential sector, where more than half of the households use natural gas as their primary energy source for home heating, uses about two-fifths of the natural gas delivered to end-users in Missouri.48 The industrial sector and the commercial sector each use about one-fourth of the state's natural gas deliveries to consumers. The electric power sector typically uses much less.49 Although the use of natural gas for power generation has fluctuated, it has increased overall in recent years, reaching a high of more than 50 billion cubic feet in 2012.50

Coal

The first commercial coal production west of the Mississippi River occurred in Missouri. By the late 1880s, coal mining in the state was a thriving industry.51 All of the state's coal is bituminous, and almost all of it has a high sulfur content.52 Although the state still produces some coal, Missouri's current coal production is modest and equals only about 1% of the coal consumed in the state.53,54

More coal is consumed for power generation in Missouri than in all but three other states.

More coal is consumed for power generation in Missouri than in all but a few other states in the nation; only Texas, Illinois, and Indiana use more.55 Most of the coal fueling Missouri's coal-fired power plants comes by rail from Wyoming. Small amounts of coal also arrive by rail and truck from other states, primarily Illinois. Some of the coal used at industrial plants in Missouri is delivered by river barges from Illinois. Additional small amounts of coal come to industrial, commercial, and institutional users in the state from Illinois, Indiana, and Colorado.56

Electricity

Coal fuels almost four-fifths of Missouri's net electricity generation, and 8 of the 10 largest power plants in the state are coal-fired.57,58 One-eighth of the electricity generation in Missouri comes from the state's single reactor at the Callaway nuclear power plant in Fulton, Missouri, and another one-twentieth comes from natural gas-fired power plants. Most of the rest of the state's net electricity generation comes from renewable energy sources, primarily hydroelectric and wind facilities.59,60

Almost all of the electricity generated in Missouri is provided by electric utilities.61 Much of the state is served by electric cooperatives and municipal utilities; however, most of the population, which is concentrated in the urban areas, receives its electric service from investor-owned utilities.62,63 The retail price of electricity in Missouri is well below the national average for all end-use sectors.64 One-third of the households in Missouri rely on electricity as their primary energy source for home heating.65

Renewable energy

Missouri ranks 12th in the nation in ethanol production capacity.

Missouri's primary renewable energy resources are hydroelectric power and wind energy.66 Missouri has several pumped storage facilities and conventional hydroelectric power plants, but the state has not fully exploited all of its hydroelectric power potential.67,68 Although Missouri's installed generating capacity from wind energy ranks 25th in the nation, the state has almost 500 megawatts of capacity online.69 Some of the state's net electricity generation is from biomass, mostly wood. Missouri has significant biomass potential from agricultural waste; municipal solid waste and landfill gas; and the 14 million acres of forest that cover roughly one-third of the state.70,71,72 A small but increasing amount of electricity generation is coming from solar photovoltaic installations in Missouri.73 However, renewable resources currently contribute less than 5% of the state's net electricity generation.74

Missouri has the fourth-largest biodiesel production capacity in the nation.

Bioenergy production in Missouri includes alternative liquid fuels in the form of ethanol and biodiesel. The state has the fourth-largest biodiesel production capacity in the nation with nine biodiesel plants, and another one under construction. The biodiesel plants in Missouri use a variety of feedstocks, mostly soy oil or animal fats.75,76 Missouri also has seven facilities that use corn, sorghum, and switchgrass to produce ethanol, and the state ranks 12th in the nation in ethanol production capacity.77,78

To promote the use of renewable energy resources, Missouri adopted a mandatory renewable energy standard in 2008. The standard requires investor-owned electric utilities to increase the percentage of electricity sales from renewable resources incrementally up to a minimum of 15% of total sales by 2021. The standard also requires increases in the contribution from solar energy up to a minimum of 0.3% of total retail electricity sales by 2021.79
