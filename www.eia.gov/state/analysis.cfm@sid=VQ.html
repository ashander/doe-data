Profile AnalysisPrint Territory Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: May 19, 2016

Overview

The U.S. Virgin Islands is shifting from fuel oil to propane to generate electricity and produce public drinking water.

The U.S. Virgin Islands (USVI), part of the Leeward Islands of the Lesser Antilles, is a U.S. territory located in the Caribbean Sea, about 600 miles southeast of Miami, Florida.1 The USVI has a total area of 134 square miles, about twice the size of Washington, DC. The islands are hilly and forested, with limited arable areas, and most food is imported.2 The largest island is St. Croix. It lies about 40 miles south of the three other inhabited islands: St. John, St. Thomas, and Water Island. Those islands are located between the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico on the west and the British Virgin Islands on the east.3

The USVI has a subtropical climate with easterly trade winds and little seasonal variation in its warm temperatures. The Caribbean hurricane season, from June to November, sometimes brings destructive storms.4 The territory has a population estimated at about 104,000 in 2015.5 Most of the USVI population is evenly divided between St. Thomas and St. Croix; fewer than 5,000 people live on St. John and Water Island.6 Tourism, trade, and service industries account for about three-fifths of the USVI's Gross Domestic Product (GDP). Government accounts for about one-fifth of the GDP. In the industrial sector, manufacturing was second only to tourism in economic importance until the February 2012 closure of the Hovensa crude oil refinery on St. Croix. Manufacturing is now focused on rum distilling, electronics, and pharmaceuticals.7 Financial and professional services and export-oriented services are growing in importance.8

Like most other Caribbean islands, the USVI has no fossil energy resources9 and imports petroleum products to meet its energy needs, including electricity and desalination of ocean water for its public water supply.10,11,12 In 2016, the USVI's electric utility is completing a $150 million project to enable use of propane as well as fuel oils in its generators, the largest such project in the Caribbean.13,14 The utility is also adding renewable resources.15,16 The USVI's economy has typically been about six times more energy-intensive than the economy in the 50 U.S. states,17 and per capita energy consumption in recent years has run about seven times higher than the average in the states.18,19 Energy efficiency is lowered by water desalination requirements; the predominance of small, simple-cycle generators; and operational constraints and power losses on the islands' two isolated electric grids.20 The government has set a goal of achieving a 60% reduction in fossil fuel demand across all consuming sectors by 2025.21

Petroleum

The USVI does not produce crude oil, and it has no known reserves,22 although a recent U.S. Geological Survey assessment identified the potential for undiscovered crude oil resources in a subsea formation south of the islands.23 The Hovensa petroleum refinery on St. Croix, one of the 10 largest crude oil refineries in the world, was a substantial part of the USVI's economy and provided most of the islands' petroleum products until it was shut down in February 2012.24 Since the closure, refined products are imported primarily through the port of Christiansted on St. Croix. Major supply sources include nations in the Caribbean Basin.25

Until recently, about two-thirds of all petroleum products in the USVI were consumed in the form of No. 6 residual fuel oil and No. 2 diesel fuel for electricity and potable water production. The remaining one-third was used almost entirely in the transportation sector, as motor gasoline, diesel fuel, and aviation fuel. Diesel fuel is also consumed by USVI residents who install generators for electricity supply.26 In 2008, world petroleum prices pushed USVI electricity rates above 50 cents per kilowatthour. The USVI government set a goal of reducing petroleum use by 60% by 2025, from nearly 2.5 million barrels per year to less than 1 million barrels.27 Through a combination of efficiency and technology upgrades in power and water production, plus introduction of renewable generation, petroleum consumption was reduced 20% by 2013.28 The electric and water utility considered converting generation to liquefied natural gas (LNG) but, in 2013, opted to convert generators to propane, which required less new infrastructure.29,30 The utility received its first propane shipment in October 2015,31 and introduction of propane as fuel at the USVI's two generating plants is scheduled to occur during 2016.32

Natural gas

The USVI does not produce natural gas and has no known reserves,33 although the recent U.S. Geological Survey assessment identified the potential for undiscovered natural gas resources in the same subsea formation south of the islands that is thought to contain petroleum resources.34 To reduce dependence on petroleum fuels, island officials have been seeking ways to access natural gas, but the islands' small energy demand makes building an LNG terminal difficult to justify economically. Prospects for LNG imports dimmed further with the shutdown of the Hovensa refinery, which could have been a major customer for LNG.35 Virgin Island authorities are looking to possible regional solutions to obtain LNG economically, working with governments of other Caribbean islands that face a similar challenge.36

Coal

The USVI does not produce or consume coal and has no known coal reserves.37

Electricity

The USVI has two separate island grids that must each maintain generation backup and reserves.

The USVI has two separate electricity grids, each with its own generation, managed by the Water and Power Authority (WAPA), an independent governmental agency. Generating units include combustion and steam turbines and backup diesel, plus a small amount of solar photovoltaic (PV) resources owned by independent power producers (IPPs). The 191-megawatt St. Thomas system supplies electricity to nearby St. John and Water Island by underwater cable. The 117-megawatt St. Croix system, separated from St. Thomas by 40 miles of ocean, has its own grid. Average loads are less than half of capacity, but both isolated systems must maintain their own generation backup and reserves.38,39 Seabed depth makes connecting the St. Thomas and St. Croix systems difficult.40 The feasibility of using cables to connect each system with Puerto Rico's larger system are being explored. Connections could lower reserve requirements and increase reliability on all the islands.41,42

Historically, all electricity generation was fueled with imported petroleum. In recent years, fuel surcharges for No. 2 diesel and No. 6 residual fuel oil resulted in USVI electricity rates up to five times higher than the average U.S. states' price for electricity.43,44,45 Even with falling world petroleum prices, the electric rate in the first half of 2016 was about three times higher.46,47 After petroleum prices soared in 2008, WAPA explored conversion to LNG48 but, in 2013, opted to build shipping and storage facilities needed to fuel its generators with propane.49 The turbine conversions for the project have given WAPA's generators fuel flexibility, allowing them to burn propane, fuel oil, or LNG.50 In 2016, WAPA is slated to complete conversion of the two main generating plants to propane.51,52,53 The two plants are the Randolph E. Harley plant near Krum Bay on St. Thomas and the Richmond plant near Christiansted on St. Croix. Both plants were aging and needed increasingly costly maintenance and upgrading,54,55 and both are under federal consent decrees to reduce emissions.56 Introduction of propane at the Richmond plant started in April 2016,57 and conversion of the Harley plant will begin once docking facilities for propane supply vessels are completed.58,59 Propane is expected to cut generating plant emissions up to 20% and enable WAPA to cut fuel surcharges by 30%.60

In the last decade, nearly one-tenth of all electricity WAPA generated was used for desalination of sea water to make public drinking water.61,62 In 2013, WAPA transitioned to more energy-efficient reverse osmosis technology to provide potable water.63,64,65 About one-fourth of the islands' population is served by the public water supply.66 Homes not connected to the public water supply are required to have cisterns to catch rainwater; a cistern may represent almost one-sixth of the cost of building a private home.67

About one-third of WAPA's electricity is typically consumed by the residential sector, and another one-third is consumed by large commercial power users like hotels. Nearly one-sixth of electricity goes to large industrial users such as distilleries and ports.68 Average household consumption is around 450 kilowatthours monthly, about half that of the 50 states. Nearly two-fifths of the USVI government's targeted 60% reduction in fossil fuel use is to come from energy efficiency,69 both in electricity generation and in consumption.70 WAPA generators have been overhauled to improve performance, street lights are being switched to energy-saving light-emitting diode (LED) bulbs, and Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) has been installed throughout the electricity distribution system.71 A WAPA contractor is developing an Integrated Resource Plan (IRP) to identify future system options, including the generation portfolio, consumers' needs, and potential regional interconnections.72

Renewable energy

The USVI government's goal of reducing fossil fuel use 60% by 2025 led to working with U.S. federal agencies and industry to find alternative energy sources, including wind, solar, and biomass technologies such as waste-to-energy and landfill gas.73,74 In 2009, the USVI legislature voted to require that 30% of WAPA's "peak demanded generating capacity" be from renewable sources by 2025, increasing thereafter until a majority of capacity is renewables-sourced.75,76 The USVI was the United States' pilot project for the international Energy Development in Island Nations (EDIN) partnership, aimed at demonstrating economic ways for islands worldwide to reduce energy costs by using efficiency and renewable technologies.77 The USVI government and WAPA took EDIN recommendations forward under a plan called VIenergize.78,79

Distributed solar generation is providing 13% of the USVI's peak power demand.

The USVI has strong solar power potential on all its islands.80 The USVI's first large solar facility was a 450-kilowatt array at King Airport on St. Thomas.81 By February 2016, 1.7% of WAPA's electric power was generated by solar power,82 primarily from two 4-megawatt facilities: Estate Donoe on St. Thomas, inaugurated in 2015,83 and Estate Spanish Town, which opened on St. Croix in 2014.84 These projects are run by independent power producers (IPPs). Two more IPP solar PV facilities, each 3 megawatts, are under construction, and the University of the Virgin Islands has contracted for a 3.3-megawatt solar PV system for its two campuses.85,86 In addition, distributed solar generation on consumer rooftops is providing about 15 megawatts, or about 13% of WAPA's daytime peak demand.87 Because of concerns about grid stability with variable renewables, the USVI's net metering law currently caps net metered connections at 10 megawatts on the St. Thomas grid and 5 megawatts on the St. Croix system.88 A study in 2012 found the islands' grids could potentially incorporate up to 45 megawatts of solar and wind generation.89 Since 1990, the government has provided rebates for consumer-sited renewable facilities like solar PV panels, solar water heaters, and small wind turbines.90 Solar water heaters are required in all new construction and in major renovations. The USVI legislature directed American Recovery and Reinvestment Act funds to rebates for solar water heating systems,91 and nearly 1,500 systems have been installed in the islands.92

The USVI has commercial wind energy potential,93,94 but finding the large sites needed for utility-scale projects has been challenging. In addition, the fluctuating output of wind generators complicates their integration into the islands' small grids, and the risk of damage from hurricanes has made financing wind projects difficult.95 The most promising locations for utility-scale wind projects are on high ridges and exposed capes.96 Wind speeds were found suitable for commercial-scale turbines at sites around Longford on St. Croix and the Bovoni Peninsula on St. Thomas.97

The USVI also has renewable potential in waste-to-energy, landfill gas, and biomass energy.98 WAPA has agreed to purchase the power output of a 7-megawatt generating plant, fed by biogas from a dedicated grass crop. The plant's developer has been negotiating financing for construction and operation on St. Croix.99,100,101,102 Studies estimate that combustion of landfill waste could provide up to 8 megawatts of generating capacity while reducing waste volumes,103,104 but proposals for waste-to-energy plants at landfills on St. Thomas and St. Croix were deemed too costly.105 A small landfill gas project was constructed at St. Thomas' Bovoni landfill.106 That landfill is operating under an Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) consent decree that currently requires closure in 2019.107,108
