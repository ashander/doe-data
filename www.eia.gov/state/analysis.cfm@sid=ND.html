Profile AnalysisPrint State Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: February 18, 2016

Overview

North Dakota's Williston Basin contains several of the nation's largest oil fields.

North Dakota has one of the smallest populations in the United States, but it experienced one of the largest percentage increases in population between 2010 and 2015, primarily because of the state's recent oil boom.1,2 The state has substantial fossil energy resources, including both crude oil and lignite coal reserves.3,4 Located at the geographic center of North America, North Dakota has a continental climate characterized by large temperature variations, irregular precipitation, plentiful sunshine, low humidity, and nearly continuous wind. North Dakota's rolling plains slope gently upward toward the Rocky Mountains in the west, and two major rivers, the Missouri River and the Red River, and their tributaries flow through the state. Construction of the Garrison Dam on the Missouri River in the 1950s significantly reduced the extent of serious flooding in the state while supplying abundant hydroelectric power. The Red River Valley, a flat glacial-lake plain, is noted for its highly productive farmland,5 and the state's productive soils supply significant amounts of corn for ethanol production, placing North Dakota among the top 10 ethanol-producing states in the nation.6,7 North Dakota's winds and abundant sunshine are additional renewable resources and are contributing increasing shares of the state's electricity generation.8,9

Total energy consumption in North Dakota ranks among the lowest in the nation; however, consumption per person and per dollar of gross domestic product (GDP) ranks among the highest because of the energy-intensive industrial sector and the high heating demand during North Dakota's very cold winters.10,11,12,13 The industrial sector accounts for nearly half of end-use energy consumption in the state.14 Mining, including the energy-intensive oil and gas industry, real estate, and agriculture are the major contributors to North Dakota's GDP.15,16 The transportation sector is the second largest energy-consuming end-use sector and accounts for almost one-fourth of energy use in the state.17

North Dakota's total energy production is about four times greater than its consumption.18 The surge in production over the past few years has come from the development of petroleum resources within the Williston Basin's Bakken Shale play.19 More than two-thirds of the state's total energy production is in the form of crude oil. Coal and natural gas provide about one-fourth of the total energy production, and renewable energy, including the rapidly developing wind sector, provides almost all of the remainder.20

Petroleum

The Williston Basin, including the prolific Bakken Shale formation, and several of the nation's largest oil fields are in North Dakota.21,22 Oil exploration in North Dakota began in the early 20th century, but the first oil discovery did not occur until 1951.23 Production was modest until the exploitation of the Bakken formation in western North Dakota in the Williston Basin, a large, roughly circular basin that includes several hundred thousand square miles of North Dakota, South Dakota, Montana, and the Canadian provinces of Manitoba and Saskatchewan.24 Crude oil production in North Dakota was more than 13 times greater in 2014 than in 2003, primarily as a result of the introduction of improved drilling and hydraulic fracturing technologies that increased production from the Bakken Shale.25,26,27 North Dakota has been the second-largest crude oil-producing state in the nation, after Texas, since 2012.28,29

There are more than 7 billion barrels of recoverable oil in the Bakken and Three Forks formations in the Williston Basin; much of that oil is in North Dakota.

North Dakota ranks second in the nation in crude oil reserves.30 In 2008, the U.S. Geological Survey estimated that the Bakken formation contained more than 3.6 billion barrels of oil, 25 times greater than their 1995 estimate.31 A revised assessment released in 2013 estimated that there are more than 7 billion barrels of technically recoverable oil from the Bakken and Three Forks formations in the Williston Basin; much of that oil is in North Dakota.32 With the opening of a new refinery in 2015, North Dakota now has two refineries, with an operable capacity of almost 93,000 barrels of crude oil per calendar day.33,34 More refineries are in development to accommodate increased Bakken and Three Forks production.35,36,37

North Dakota is crossed by more than a dozen crude oil and petroleum product pipelines.38,39 The state is a U.S. entrance point for pipelines carrying Canadian crude oil. Some Canadian crude oil is transported via pipeline east to Midwest refining markets and south to Cushing, Oklahoma, and to Port Arthur, Texas.40 Producers in North Dakota ship about half of their crude oil by rail because oil production in the region has outpaced pipeline capacity.41

North Dakota consumes relatively little of the petroleum and ethanol produced there.42,43,44 More than half of the petroleum used in North Dakota is consumed by the transportation sector. Statewide use of conventional motor gasoline without ethanol is allowed, although there are more than 50 fueling stations in the state that distribute E85, a blend of 85% ethanol and 15% gasoline.45,46,47 The industrial sector accounts for slightly more than one-third of the petroleum consumed in the state.48 Less than 5% of the petroleum used in North Dakota is consumed by the residential sector, where about one-sixth of households use petroleum products, including fuel oil, liquefied petroleum gas (LPG), and kerosene, for home heating.49,50

Natural gas

Natural gas production in North Dakota, although less than 1.5% of the nation's total, has been increasing rapidly since 2007.51 Natural gas was discovered in the state as early as 1892, decades before oil was. Early natural gas production supplied several nearby towns at the beginning of the 20th century. Significant commercial production was not established until 1929, when development of a Montana natural gas field extended into North Dakota. Discovery and development of natural gas fields in North Dakota continued sporadically from the mid-1940s until the early 1980s.52 After peaking in the mid-1980s, production did not increase significantly until 2007 with the production of natural gas from shales.53

North Dakota has no underground natural gas storage fields.54 Several natural gas pipelines cross the state, and the North Dakota Pipeline Authority is seeking ways to encourage expansion of the state's energy transportation infrastructure to accommodate the increased production.55,56 Although recent low natural gas prices provided little incentive for building new natural gas pipelines elsewhere, the natural gas produced in North Dakota is rich in natural gas liquids (NGL); attractive NGL prices resulted in the development of new natural gas processing facilities and pipelines in the state.57 Those projects substantially increased natural gas-processing capacity and pipeline access in the region.58 However, North Dakota natural gas production still exceeds pipeline capacity and natural gas has been flared (burned) at the wellhead. North Dakota's Industrial Commission has established targets to decrease the amount of flared natural gas over the next several years.59

North Dakota typically accounts for about 0.3% of the nation's natural gas consumption.60 Interstate deliveries of natural gas enter North Dakota primarily from Montana and Canada and continue on to South Dakota and Minnesota. More natural gas leaves the state than enters because of the added North Dakota production.61 About one-third of the natural gas consumed in North Dakota is used in the production and transportation of natural gas. Of the remaining two-thirds, half is consumed in the industrial sector. The commercial and the residential sectors each use about one-fourth of the natural gas delivered to end users.62 Two-fifths of North Dakota households use natural gas for home heating.63

Coal

North Dakota contains the single largest known deposit of lignite in the world.

Western North Dakota contains the single largest known deposit of lignite in the world.64 Although all of its coal is relatively low grade, North Dakota is among the 10 top coal-producing states in terms of tonnage.65 Coal has been mined at hundreds of sites in North Dakota since the 1870s, but it is now recovered from only four surface mines in the west-central part of the state.66,67 Two smaller mining operations in North Dakota extract oxidized lignite (leonardite), which is used in soil stabilization and as a drilling fluid additive.68

The lignite from North Dakota's four coal mines fuels electricity generating plants in the state.69 The state's power plants also receive small amounts of coal from Montana and Wyoming.70 In addition, North Dakota uses coal to produce synthetic natural gas. The only operating coal-to-SNG (synthetic natural gas) facility in the nation is the Great Plains Synfuels Plant in Beulah, North Dakota, where lignite coal is converted into pipeline-quality gas.71,72

Electricity

Coal-fired power plants provide three-fourths of North Dakota's electricity generation.73 The rest of the state's electricity generation comes primarily from wind energy and, to a lesser extent, hydroelectric power.74 The state does not have any nuclear power plants.75 About one-eighth of the utility-scale electricity generation in North Dakota comes from independent power producers, all of which is from wind.76

Both electricity generation and consumption are low in North Dakota, and the state produces more electricity than it consumes. Slightly more than half of North Dakota's total electricity supply (which includes some net electricity imports from Canada) is provided to the interstate electricity trade.77 Two high-voltage direct current lines carry electricity east into Minnesota, and additional transmission lines carry electricity to South Dakota and beyond.78,79,80

The reliability of the North Dakota power industry is overseen by the Midwest Reliability Organization, one of the eight regional entities of the North American Electric Reliability Corporation.81 The Midwest Independent Transmission System Operator, Inc. operates much of the state's electricity grid.82 Rural electric cooperatives and investor-owned utilities serve the largest number of North Dakota's retail customers. Municipal utilities serve the rest.83

Renewable energy

North Dakota has abundant renewable resources, many of which are not fully developed. With average wind speeds ranging from 10 miles per hour to 13 miles per hour depending on location, the air is rarely calm in North Dakota.84 The state ranks fifth in the nation in the percentage of its electricity generated by wind energy and has substantial undeveloped wind energy potential.85,86 North Dakota has achieved rapid gains in wind-powered electricity generation. Almost 260 megawatts of utility-scale generating capacity from wind came online in North Dakota in 2015.87

North Dakota's other renewable resources include biofuels and hydroelectric power. The state has several ethanol plants that use corn as feedstock. The combined capacity of those plants is more than 470 million gallons of ethanol per year.88 The Garrison Dam, northwest of Bismarck, is the site of North Dakota's only hydroelectric plant, and it is the fifth largest power plant in the state in terms of generating capacity.89,90 Renewable resource potential in the state includes geothermal and solar energy as well. The western half of North Dakota has moderately favorable conditions for geothermal development.91 Although there is only a small amount of solar energy electricity generation in the state, North Dakota has abundant sunshine.92

In March 2007, North Dakota adopted a voluntary renewable portfolio objective that set a goal for electricity generated from renewable sources of 10% of all retail electricity sold in the state by 2015. The voluntary goal applies to all retail providers of electricity. Eligible resources include electricity produced by solar, wind, biomass, hydropower, geothermal, hydrogen derived from another eligible resource, and recycled energy systems that generate electricity from currently unused waste heat. A final progress report from each participating electricity provider is due in June 2016.93,94

Energy on Tribal Lands

North Dakota is home to all or part of some of the largest reservations in the nation, but only about 2% of the state is in tribal hands.95,96 The approximately 40,000 Native Americans living in North Dakota are members of several Plains Indian tribes.97,98 Those tribes share five reservations, two of which straddle the state line between North Dakota and South Dakota.99

The Native American tribes of North Dakota have shared in the state's oil boom. Crude oil production from Indian lands in the United States has increased more than four-fold between 2003 and 2014, and much of the new production has come from wells on North Dakota tribal lands. The Fort Berthold Indian Reservation, home to the Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara nations—known as the Three Affiliated Tribes—is in the prolific oil-producing Williston Basin in the western part of North Dakota.100 Much of North Dakota's oil reserves are beneath the Affiliated Tribes reservation, and the wells there account for almost one-fifth of North Dakota's daily oil production.101 Although more than 1,300 oil wells have been drilled on the Fort Berthold Indian Reservation by non-tribal oil companies, none have been drilled by the tribes. The Affiliated Tribes plan to drill the first tribe-owned wells on the reservation, two targeting the Bakken formation and two targeting the Three Forks formation.102 The nation's first tribal refinery began construction on the Fort Berthold reservation in 2013.103 A crude oil pipeline that would cross the Fort Berthold reservation was granted a permit in January 2016.104 Natural gas, a byproduct of oil production, has been flared at the wellhead because of a lack of natural gas pipelines in North Dakota's oil fields. A proposed pipeline, a short section of which crosses Fort Berthold tribal land, would transport natural gas to markets and reduce the state's oilfield flaring of natural gas by an estimated 10%.105,106

North Dakota reservations have significant renewable resources. The Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa is forming a tribal electric utility to become more energy independent and is updating a wind turbine development project on the reservation.107 The Standing Rock Sioux reservation of North Dakota and South Dakota is rated among the top five reservations in the nation in terms of its potential for wind- and solar photovoltaic-powered electricity generation. The Lake Traverse Reservation, a small portion of which is in North Dakota, is a leader among the nation's reservations in its potential for electricity generation from biomass.108
