Arkansas State Energy Profile



Arkansas Quick Facts

  • Marketed natural gas production in Arkansas experienced a nearly six-fold increase between 2005 and 2014. In 2014 it accounted for 4.1% of U.S. marketed production.
  • Arkansas, the top rice-producing state as of 2014, typically experiences an increase in natural gas consumption in the industrial sector (which includes agriculture) in the fall when natural gas is used to dry rice after harvest.
  • Coal-fired electric power plants in Arkansas supplied over half (54%) of the state's electricity in 2014 and relied on coal deliveries via railcar from Wyoming.
  • Independent power producers provided over 18% of net electricity generation in Arkansas in 2014.
  • Biomass supplied all of Arkansas' non-hydroelectric renewable energy resources for utility-scale electricity generation in 2014.

Analysis

Last Updated: January 21, 2016

Overview

Forests cover more than half of Arkansas and provide significant woody biomass resource potential.

Arkansas is bounded to the east by the Mississippi River, to the west by the Great Plains, and to the south by the Gulf Coastal Plain. The Mississippi River Valley and the upper reaches of its delta occupy the eastern portion of the state and have created rich soils, which, with Arkansas' hot, humid summers and mild, slightly drier winters, provide excellent conditions for agriculture.1,2,3 Forests cover more than half of the state, providing significant woody biomass resource potential.4 The Gulf Coastal Plain, which extends into southern and eastern Arkansas, contains historically significant oil-producing geologic formations.5 The Ouachita Mountains and the Ozark Mountains form the Interior Highlands of northern and western Arkansas. Those mountain ranges are separated by the fertile Arkansas River Valley and the Arkansas portion of the natural gas-rich Arkoma Basin.6,7

Arkansas produces more total energy than it consumes, primarily because of its abundant natural gas resources, but the state's per capita total energy consumption is in the top one-third of all of the states in the nation.8,9 The industrial sector, which includes agriculture, is the end-use sector that consumes the most energy in Arkansas, followed by the transportation sector.10 Rice and poultry farming are two of the state's most important agricultural products, and food production leads the manufacturing sector in terms of revenue. The energy-intensive forest products industry, which includes the manufacture of paper products, ranks second, followed closely by the manufacture of fabricated metal products.11,12 Arkansas has a relatively mild climate, but changes in terrain and increases in elevation from the Gulf Coastal Plain to the Interior Highlands can cause weather extremes.13 Most households heat with electricity or natural gas, although almost 1 in 8 uses liquefied petroleum gas or wood for heating.14

Petroleum

Arkansas led the nation's crude oil production in the 1920s with the discovery of the Smackover oil field.

The commercial petroleum industry in Arkansas began in the 1920s, and crude oil production has been concentrated in the southern part of the state since then.15,16 Although many Arkansas oil wells are now stripper wells that produce less than 10 barrels of crude oil per day, the state has produced almost 2 billion barrels of oil since 1920 and still produces almost 7 million barrels of oil per year.17,18 Some of the wells in Arkansas are remnants of two giant oil fields discovered in the first half of the twentieth century. Arkansas led the nation's crude oil production in the 1920s with the discovery of the Smackover oil field. A second giant oil field, the Magnolia field, was discovered in 1938.19

Arkansas has two oil refineries that have a combined operable capacity of about 90,000 barrels of crude oil per calendar day.20,21 The largest refinery in the state, the El Dorado Refinery, can process more than 80,000 barrels of sour crude oil per calendar day. Its product line includes ultra-low sulfur gasoline and diesel fuels, propane, solvents, and asphalt products.22,23 To offset declines in production from local Arkansas producers and to take advantage of increased refinery capacity, other foreign and offshore domestic crude oils are also processed at the El Dorado Refinery.24 A much smaller refinery, in Smackover, Arkansas, processes about 7,500 barrels per calendar day.25

Two major crude oil pipelines cross Arkansas, but one of them ruptured in March 2013. The subsequent oil spill has shut down the Arkansas section of that pipeline.26 Three petroleum product pipelines also cross the state.27 The state does not have any ethanol plants, and Arkansas allows the statewide use of conventional motor gasoline.28,29 The transportation sector accounts for four-fifths of the petroleum consumed in Arkansas.30

Natural gas

Arkansas is among the top 10 natural gas-producing states.

Arkansas is one of the top 10 natural gas producers in the nation. Dry natural gas production in the state experienced a six-fold increase in the past decade, peaking in 2012 at about 4% of the nation's total.31 Proved natural gas reserves in Arkansas also increased almost six-fold in the same period, peaking in 2011 when they accounted for about 5% of the U.S. total.32,33 Some of the increase is the result of horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing in the Fayetteville Shale, the current focus of shale gas exploration in Arkansas.34,35 Coalbed methane is also produced in Arkansas. The development of this resource began in 2001. Although the state is not a major producer of coalbed methane, coal gas wells yielded more than 27 billion cubic feet of natural gas from 2001 to 2014.36,37 Most of the state's natural gas production is from the Arkoma Basin, although there are several wells in the Gulf Coastal Plain.38

Arkansas produces about four times more natural gas than its residents consume.39,40 The state has no natural gas market centers. Several major natural gas pipelines from Louisiana, Oklahoma, and Texas pass through Arkansas on the way to markets in the Midwest and Northeast.41 Large volumes of natural gas flow into the state and even larger volumes move out, mostly through Mississippi and Missouri on their way east and north.42 Typically, about two-thirds of the natural gas consumed in Arkansas is used by the industrial and electric power sectors.43 Consumption for electric power generation rose sharply in the past decade, peaking in 2012, and the electric power sector was the state's leading natural gas-consumer from 2009 through 2013.44,45 In 2014, the industrial sector once again became the leading consumer of natural gas in the state.46 Two-fifths of Arkansas households use natural gas as their primary source for home heating.47

Coal

There are two small bituminous coal mines in Arkansas, both in the Arkansas River Valley near the state's western border.48,49 The Arkansas mines supply less than 1% of the state's coal needs, and Arkansas relies on other states for supply. Almost all of the coal consumed in Arkansas is used by the electric power sector.50,51 Coal brought into the state for electricity generation comes by rail from Wyoming. Industrial facilities in Arkansas obtain coal from Illinois by truck and from Oklahoma by rail and truck.52

Electricity

Coal is the primary fuel used for electricity generation in Arkansas.

Coal is the primary fuel used for electricity generation in Arkansas and accounts for about half of the electricity produced in the state.53 The state's only nuclear generating station typically provides between one-fifth and one-fourth of the state's net generation.54,55 Although 5 of the 10 largest power plants by capacity in Arkansas use coal, the largest power plant in the state is natural gas-fired.56 Natural gas, which at its peak in 2012 fueled more than one-fourth of the state's generation, contributed only one-sixth of the state's total net generation in 2014.57,58,59 Independent power producers in Arkansas primarily use natural gas. A small amount of power is generated by the industrial sector using biomass. Hydroelectric facilities also contribute to the state's electricity supply.60

The residential sector accounts for two-fifths of electricity sales in Arkansas. Retail sales to the residential sector are greater than sales to the industrial sector or to the commercial sector.61 Almost half of the households in the state use electricity as their primary energy source for home heating.62

Renewable energy

Hydroelectric and biomass-fueled facilities provide about 7% of electricity generation in Arkansas.

Hydroelectric power is the most commonly used renewable resource in Arkansas, providing almost two-thirds of the state's renewable power generation. Almost all of the hydroelectric generation is supplied by electric utilities. Biomass supplies all of Arkansas' nonhydroelectric renewable energy for electricity generation, almost all of it from wood and wood-derived fuels.63,64 Solid biomass residues and methane from livestock, a major portion of the state's agricultural base, are other potential sources.65 There are three wood pellet plants in Arkansas and more in development.66 The state also has several facilities that produce biofuels from animal fats and vegetable oils.67 Arkansas does not have any utility-scale electricity generation from wind energy projects; however, a number of manufacturers of the components used by wind generators are located in the state.68 Arkansas has a small but increasing amount of installed solar generating capacity.69 Overall, renewable resources provide about 7% of the state's electricity generation.70

Arkansas does not have a renewable portfolio standard.71 The state has adopted several policies to encourage energy efficiency. In 2009, the Sustainable Energy-Efficient Buildings Program was enacted, directing the Arkansas Energy Office to develop a plan for reducing energy use in all major existing state-owned facilities by 30% from 2008 levels by 2017.72 Also in 2009, the Arkansas Alternative Energy Commission was created to study the need for and impact of various forms of alternative energy in Arkansas.73 In December 2010, the Arkansas Public Service Commission announced a Sustainable Energy Resource Action Plan requiring implementation of energy efficiency measures by the state's investor-owned utilities.74
