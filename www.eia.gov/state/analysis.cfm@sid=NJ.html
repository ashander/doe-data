Profile AnalysisPrint State Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: July 21, 2016

Overview

New Jersey is a major distribution center for petroleum products to the northeastern United States.

New Jersey is home to beaches, marshes, highlands, and major suburbs and port facilities serving the neighboring Philadelphia and New York City metropolitan areas. The climate is temperate but variable. Weather in coastal areas is moderated by the ocean. Weather inland is influenced by urbanization and, in the north, by the Appalachian Mountains.1

New Jersey has the highest population density of any state in the nation.2,3 Many New Jersey residents commute to work in the New York City or Philadelphia metropolitan areas, and the state has some of the nation's longest commute times.4 Shipping complexes along the Delaware River and in the New York-New Jersey harbor—with their connecting pipeline, rail, and air terminals—make New Jersey a major distribution center for petroleum products for the Northeast.5

New Jersey is a major consumer of petroleum products,6 and the petroleum-dependent transportation sector consumes more energy than any other sector in the state.7 New Jersey depends on nuclear power and natural gas for most of its net electricity generation.8 The state's industrial sector energy consumption is below the national median despite its energy-intensive chemical manufacturing and petroleum refining industries.9 Overall, New Jersey ranks in the lowest one-fifth of states in energy consumed per dollar of gross domestic product.10

Petroleum

New Jersey does not produce crude oil,11 but the state has three operating oil refineries.12 Two complex refineries produce a range of refined products, including motor gasoline, diesel fuel, and heating oil,13,14 and one refinery produces asphalt.15 When the cost of imported crude oil feedstock rose in recent years, refinery owners in New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware developed rail shipping capability for crude oil feedstock from Canada and North Dakota's Bakken Shale to enable continued operations.16,17 The refineries can now take feedstock by rail or tanker, whichever is more economic.18 Three other New Jersey refineries have been shut down since 2009.19 Some shutdown refinery sites have been converted into petroleum storage terminals, taking advantage of the pipeline, rail, road, and marine facilities already on location.20,21

A large part of New York Harbor lies on the New Jersey shore. By 2008, the harbor had petroleum bulk terminal storage capacity of more than 75 million barrels, making it the largest petroleum product hub in the Northeast.22,23 In 2011, the federal government concluded that the area had enough storage capacity and that a regional stockpile of 1 million barrels of home heating oil in Perth Amboy, New Jersey, part of the federal Northeast Home Heating Oil Reserve, was no longer needed. The reserve's two remaining storage centers, in Connecticut and Massachusetts, were converted to ultra-low sulfur diesel (ULSD) following decisions made by several states, including New Jersey, to begin requiring ULSD for home heating fuel.24 Slightly more than 1 in 10 New Jersey households depend on fuel oil as their primary source of home heating.25

The largest U.S. petroleum products pipeline from the Gulf Coast region terminates in New Jersey.

New Jersey is crossed by major petroleum product pipeline systems. Linden, New Jersey, is the northern terminus of the Colonial Pipeline, the nation's largest product pipeline and a critical supplier to the New York and New England markets. The Colonial Pipeline system has been expanded to increase the supply of finished petroleum products to the Northeast after several regional refineries shut down.26 Other pipeline systems distribute refined petroleum products from New Jersey refineries and terminals west to Pennsylvania and upstate New York.27 New Jersey also receives petroleum product imports by tanker and by barge, mainly from Canada, Europe, Latin America, Africa, and India.28

New Jersey is one of the few states that require statewide use of reformulated motor gasoline blended with ethanol.29 The New York Harbor area is the primary regional distribution hub for ethanol supplies.30 Several petroleum logistics firms have facilities in New Jersey to receive ethanol shipments by unit train from the Midwest and by ship from other countries. The ethanol is distributed throughout the Northeast for blending with motor gasoline.31

Natural gas

In New Jersey, natural gas is used primarily by the electric power and residential sectors.32 About three-fourths of households in the state use natural gas for home heating.33 New Jersey's natural gas has traditionally come from the Gulf of Mexico region.34 The state is crossed by five major interstate pipelines that are primary carriers of natural gas from the Gulf region into New York and New England,35 and about three-fifths of the natural gas entering New Jersey travels on to other states.36 New pipeline sections are being proposed and built to transport more natural gas from Pennsylvania's nearby Marcellus Shale into the Northeast,37 and distribution infrastructure within the state is being upgraded.38 In 2015, new infrastructure enabled the opening of two new electricity generating plants fueled with natural gas in the state.39

New Jersey has no proved natural gas reserves40,41 and does not have any natural gas production.42 Controversy over hydraulic fracturing in the Marcellus Shale in neighboring states has spilled over to New Jersey because of shared concern about protecting the quality of Delaware River Basin drinking water. Although no drilling has been proposed in the state, New Jersey banned hydraulic fracturing for the year 2012, and the legislature has passed bills barring disposal of drilling wastes.43

Coal

New Jersey does not have any coal mining.44 The state's coal-fired electricity generating plants receive coal by rail, usually from Pennsylvania, West Virginia, and Virginia,45 and have also imported coal from Latin America and Asia.46 New Jersey is obtaining less than 5% of its net electricity generation from coal, down from one-fifth in the 1990s.47,48 Most of its coal-fired power plants have been shut down, converted to natural gas, or added dual-firing capability. Current electric utility plans leave just two stations, which both cogenerate industrial steam, using coal as their only fuel.49,50,51

Electricity

In 2015, for the first time, natural gas supplied more of New Jersey's net electricity generation than nuclear power. Between them, the two fuels supplied more than nine-tenths of the power generated in the state.52,53 Through 2014, nuclear power typically supplied about half of New Jersey's net electricity generation.54,55,56 The state has three nuclear power plants, including the Oyster Creek Nuclear Generating Station, the oldest operating nuclear power plant in the United States. Oyster Creek came online in 1969 and is scheduled to be shut permanently in 2019.57 However, new generation is primarily natural-gas fired, and some coal-fired plants have been converted to natural gas, increasing natural gas-fired electricity generation by nearly half between 2011 and 2015.58,59 Coal and renewables provide less than one-tenth of in-state electricity generation.60

Electricity consumption in New Jersey is declining, and the state obtains just under one-tenth of its power from generators in other states.61,62 The commercial sector uses half of all electricity consumed in the state, and the residential sector consumes two-fifths.63 Only one in nine New Jersey households use electricity as their primary heat source.64 New Jersey's average electricity prices are typically among the 10 highest of the 50 states.65 The state restructured its power industry in 1999 and allows customers to choose retail electricity suppliers.66 About one in six customers has opted for non-utility suppliers.67

Renewable energy

In 2015, solar power supplied two-thirds of New Jersey's net renewable electricity generation.

Renewable energy supplies less than 5% of New Jersey's net electricity generation. Solar power became the state's leading renewable energy source in 2015, supplying two-thirds of net renewable electricity generation. Previously, biomass—principally municipal solid waste and landfill gas—had been the largest renewable power provider, and, in 2015, biomass facilities supplied nearly all the rest of the state's net renewable electricity generation.68

New Jersey's renewable portfolio standard (RPS) was initiated in 1999 as part of electricity regulatory restructuring.69 Since 1999, the state legislature has enacted several substantial revisions to the RPS,70 including adding specific minimum requirements for solar energy71 and for offshore wind energy.72,73 Overall, the law currently requires nearly one-fourth of the electricity sold in New Jersey after 2021 to come from qualified renewable sources. Requirements for additional solar power continue to increase until 2027.74,75

The New Jersey legislature accelerated the RPS solar requirements in 2012.76,77 By February 2016, more than 43,000 solar photovoltaic (PV) facilities were installed around the state on residential and business rooftops, with solar capacity totaling nearly 1,267 megawatts from distributed generation and 377 megawatts from utility-scale generation.78 In 2015, about three-fifths of all net solar electricity generation in New Jersey came from distributed facilities.79 Commercial solar PV farms also have been built, and the state's two largest farms each have capacities of 19.9 megawatts.80 At the end of 2015, New Jersey was fourth among the states in installed solar PV capacity.81

Only a small fraction of New Jersey's renewable electricity is generated by wind,82 at two facilities located on the Atlantic Ocean coast.83 New Jersey's best wind power potential is found offshore along its coastline,84 and New Jersey was the first state to establish a specific requirement for offshore wind,85 mandating 1,100 megawatts by 2021. Offshore wind projects proposed for state and federal waters off the New Jersey coast are still in the planning stages.86
