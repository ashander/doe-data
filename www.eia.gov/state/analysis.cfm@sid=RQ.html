Profile AnalysisPrint Territory Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: April 5, 2016

Overview

Puerto Ricans use one-third of the energy per capita that Americans in the states use.

The Commonwealth of Puerto Rico consists of the easternmost islands of the Greater Antilles in the Caribbean Sea, southeast of Florida. The commonwealth's main island, Puerto Rico, has coastal plains, sandy beaches, and a forested and mountainous interior, with the highest peak exceeding 4,000 feet. Rivers flowing down the mountains offer hydropower potential.1 The tropical marine climate has little seasonal variation; daytime high temperatures average about 80 degrees Fahrenheit all year. Rain occurs year round but tends to be more frequent in summer and fall. The hurricane season runs from June to November.2 The population is concentrated on the main island of Puerto Rico, with smaller populations on the islands of Vieques and Culebra. There are smaller uninhabited islands scattered around the main island.3 The commonwealth's population has been close to 4 million, but it is decreasing.4 Puerto Ricans are U.S. citizens, and emigration, primarily to the U.S. mainland, has increased since the ongoing recession began in 2006.5,6

Agriculture, mainly sugar cane, dominated Puerto Rico's economy until the middle of the 20th century.7 Although rum is still produced, sugar cane production had ceased in Puerto Rico until a recent government program reintroduced it,8,9 and agriculture has become a minor part of the economy.10 Manufacturing leads the private sector,11 with industries that include pharmaceuticals and medical devices, electronics, aviation, and renewable energy.12 Manufacturing has been attracted to Puerto Rico in part by special federal and commonwealth tax benefits.13,14,15 Tourism is also important to the economy, as is the islands' government, which employs one in five workers.16,17

Puerto Rico produces no conventional fuels. Like most Caribbean islands, the commonwealth relies heavily on imported petroleum to meet its energy needs.18 On average, the islands' residents use much less energy than consumers in the 50 U.S. states. Puerto Rico's energy intensitythe energy expended per dollar of gross domestic productis less than two-thirds of the states' energy intensity,19,20 and the commonwealth's energy consumption per capita is about one-third of the states' figure.21

Petroleum

About four-fifths of the energy used in Puerto Rico comes from petroleum.22,23 The islands neither produce nor refine crude oil. All petroleum products consumed in Puerto Rico are imported,24,25 shipped in principally through the ports of San Juan, Guayanilla, Fajardo, and Ponce.26 The last of Puerto Rico's five petroleum refineries, located at Yabucoa, was shut down in 2009,27 joining four other island refineries that were closed between 1992 and 2005.28 Storage and shipping facilities on the Yabucoa site continue to be used as a refined products terminal.29

The transportation and electric power sectors are the islands' largest petroleum consumers.30 Puerto Rico allows use of conventional motor gasoline,31,32 which makes up about 45% of annual petroleum product imports. Until 2012, imports of residual and distillate fuel oils, used primarily for electricity generation, comprised half of annual petroleum product imports. With the substitution of natural gas capability at one major generating station, that figure dropped to about two-fifths of imports.33 About 40% of Puerto Rico's electricity generating capacity is now fired with No. 6 residual fuel oil and 31% with No. 2 diesel fuel.34 To reduce both costs of and emissions from petroleum fuels, the commonwealth's electric utility is considering substituting propane or natural gas at several generating plants.35 Other petroleum products imported include jet fuel, distillate fuels, and small amounts of liquefied petroleum fuels and kerosene.36 Despite the commonwealth's low per capita total energy consumption, Puerto Rico's per capita petroleum consumption is about three-fourths of the average for the 50 states,37,38,39 primarily because of the commonwealth's dependence on residual fuel oil and diesel fuel for half of the islands' electricity.40

Natural gas

Puerto Rico does not produce natural gas.41 All natural gas is imported as liquefied natural gas (LNG) through the Peuelas terminal and regasification facility at Guayanilla Bay on the southwestern coast.42,43 The facility was built to supply the adjacent 507-megawatt EcoElctrica generating plant.44 LNG is imported mainly from Trinidad and Tobago, but recently some cargos have come from Nigeria.45 Small amounts of LNG from the U.S. mainland have also begun entering Puerto Rico in standardized cryogenic containers to supply fuel to a few industrial customers,46 but otherwise natural gas is consumed only in electricity production. Puerto Rico's per capita natural gas consumption is about one-fifth of the average in the 50 states.47,48,49 Beginning in 2012, LNG imports through Peuelas increased to support the Puerto Rico Electric Power Authority's (PREPA) conversion of the nearby oil-fired Costa Sur (South Coast) generating station to dual-fuel capability with natural gas.50,51 The U.S. Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) has approved a truck-loading facility at the terminal so that LNG can also be used in transportation.52

Puerto Rico has increased LNG imports to replace petroleum in electricity generation.

PREPA is planning to add more natural gas generation capability in order to lower fuel costs and meet federal emissions standards.53,54,55,56 PREPA plans originally called for a pipeline to take natural gas from the Peuelas terminal to the north coast, where three PREPA oil-fired generating stations are located.57 But public opposition arose to routing the pipeline through Puerto Rico's rugged central mountains,58,59 and the plan was dropped after officials found the terminal could not handle all of PREPA's expected natural gas needs.60 PREPA subsequently contracted for installation of a floating terminal with regasification capability 4 miles offshore from the Aguirre generating station on the southern coast.61 The FERC approved the facility in 2015,62 and PREPA has been negotiating financial arrangements to enable the Aguirre facility to go forward.63 PREPA is also considering the feasibility of transporting natural gas from the Aguirre terminal to its north coast generating plants or building a second LNG regasification and storage terminal on the north coast.64

Coal

Puerto Rico produces no coal.65 The island has one coal-fired electricity generating plant, at Guayama.66 The plant uses circulating fluidized-bed combustion and began operation in 2002. Typically, about 1.6 million short tons of coal are imported annually from Colombia to supply the 454-megawatt plant.67,68,69,70 Ash from coal combustion is recycled on site into a partially solidified aggregate that is used in asphalt and concrete for road construction and other applications.71,72 The commonwealth's per capita coal consumption is about one-fifth of per capita coal consumption in the 50 states.73,74

Electricity

Puerto Rico's electricity is supplied by PREPA, a government agency that owns the electricity distribution system for the main island, Vieques, and Culebra, as well as most generating stations.75 PREPA (in Spanish, Autoridad de Energa Elctrica, or AEE) was founded in the 1920s as a government irrigation system, but its responsibilities grew over the years to encompass island electrification.76 Until recently, Puerto Rico obtained two-thirds of its electricity from petroleum, generated mainly at six PREPA stations with steam turbines, combustion turbines, and/or combined cycle technology. The other one-third of PREPA's power supply was almost evenly divided between natural gas and coal generation, provided by two independent power producers, plus a small fraction from hydroelectric generators.77,78 In 2012, natural gas firing capability was added to PREPA's Costa Sur generating station in Guayanilla. As a result, in 2015, petroleum supplied half of the island's electricity, and natural gas supplied nearly one-third. Coal continued to supply about one-sixth of electricity, while renewables supplied about 2%.79,80

The cost of imported petroleum has driven Puerto Rican power prices to more than twice the average power price in the states.

PREPA gets nearly half its electricity revenues from commercial sector consumption and slightly more than one-third from residential consumption.81,82 Per capita, Puerto Rico's electricity consumption is about two-fifths of the average in the 50 states.83,84,85 In recent years, high world petroleum prices have driven typical Puerto Rican power prices to more than twice the U.S. states' average retail price.86 In 2015, PREPA electricity rates declined each month along with international petroleum prices, but, in December 2015, PREPA rates were still higher than rates in 49 of the 50 states.87 Only Hawaii's rates were higher.88 To reduce fuel costs, PREPA has planned to add natural gas capability at its largest generating stations,89 but conversions beyond Costa Sur depend on construction of LNG import terminals and natural gas distribution infrastructure.90 Plans are under way for conversion of PREPA's largest generating station, Aguirre, to use LNG supplied from a floating off-shore terminal.91 PREPA is considering a variety of alternative fuel and supply options to reduce costs and emissions at its two large San Juan-area plants, San Juan and Palo Seco, as well as options for its diesel-fueled turbines at Cambalache and Mayagez.92,93,94

PREPA is required, under both federal and commonwealth laws, to reduce plant emissions and improve generating efficiency.95 In 2014, Puerto Rico's legislature passed Law 57, reforming the commonwealth's electricity sector and creating the Energy Commission of Puerto Rico. The commission is the first commonwealth body to regulate PREPA, along with all other electricity suppliers. The commission approves wholesale and retail rates, sets efficiency and interconnection standards, and oversees PREPA'S compliance with Puerto Rico's 2010 renewable portfolio standard (RPS). The law also required PREPA to expedite connections for distributed generation, improve the efficiency of fossil generation by 60% within three years, and initiate integrated resource planning (IRP) with full public input.96

Puerto Rico, the nearby U.S. Virgin Islands, and U.S. federal agencies have explored the potential for connecting the isolated island grids with each other and with other Caribbean islands, with an eye toward a possible Caribbean-wide grid.97 A larger grid could lower costs and enable the integration of more renewable energy,98 but laying cables across deep ocean trenches would be costly, and the option is not included in PREPA's 2015 draft IRP.99

Renewable energy

In 2015, about 2% of PREPA's electricity came from renewable energy sources, with nearly two-thirds of that from wind. The rest came from solar photovoltaic (PV), hydroelectric, and landfill gas facilities.100 Puerto Rico has two wind farms, including the 95-megawatt Santa Isabel facility, the largest wind farm in the Caribbean, as well as four utility-scale solar PV installations and two landfill gas plants.101 The landfill gas plants began generating electricity late in 2015. In March 2016, ground was broken at Isabela for a 45-megawatt facility that will be the Caribbean's largest solar farm.102,103 Puerto Rico's 21 hydroelectric generating units, which were built beginning in 1915, are sited on reservoirs that often supply drinking and irrigation water as well as electricity.104,105,106,107

In 2010, Puerto Rico's legislature enacted a renewable portfolio standard (RPS) that required PREPA to get 12% of its electricity from renewable sources starting in 2015, scaling up to 15% by 2020 and 20% by 2035. Technologies meeting the standard include hydroelectric, solar PV, wind, geothermal, biomass including municipal solid waste, and ocean and tidal energy generation.108,109 PREPA determined that there was neither enough time to build new renewable facilities nor enough flexibility on its generating system to integrate the renewables needed to meet the 2015 RPS target. PREPA has proposed reduced goals of 10% renewable electricity by 2020, 12% by 2025, and 15% by 2035. In its Integrated Resource Plan (IRP), PREPA is proposing capital investment in conventional generating resources so more variable renewable sources from independent power producers can be accommodated on the island grid.110

PREPA is focusing on solar projects to deliver the renewable energy mandated by Puerto Rico's RPS.

To comply with the RPS, PREPA has focused on wind, solar, and waste-to-energy projects. The utility has signed long-term power purchase agreements with renewable energy developers for about 1,600 megawatts of renewable capacity, but not all projects have gone forward. Seventeen solar projects totaling 433 megawatts are currently considered the most likely to be completed. In 2014, PREPA renegotiated solar contracts to require energy storage equal to 30% of project capacity to increase grid stability.111,112 Multiple wind projects have been proposed, but Puerto Rico's onshore wind resource is limited, and proposed sites have faced substantial local opposition.113,114

Puerto Rico's RPS does not include any credit for distributed generation on the PREPA system. Installation of distributed solar PV facilities has risen rapidly.115 From July 2012 to April 2015, the number of customers with distributed generation quadrupled, and generation capacity increased by a factor of nine. Solar capacity at utility-scale installations totaled 52.1 megawatts and distributed solar generating capacity reached 36.9 megawatts.116 There is no legal limit on the total capacity that may be connected to PREPA's transmission system, and continued growth of distributed generation is expected.117 Puerto Rico also requires solar hot water heaters in all new single-family housing units.118 The commonwealth used funds from the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act for a weatherization assistance program that installed more than 11,000 solar hot water heaters in existing homes.119

The first two generators using landfill gas, located at Fajardo and Toa Baja, began generating electricity in late 2015.120 To encourage development of municipal solid waste, landfill gas, and other waste-to-energy facilities, PREPA has signed several power purchase agreements with developers. Proposed facilities have run into local opposition,121,122,123,124 but waste-to-energy projects have taken on new urgency since the Puerto Rico Environmental Quality Board issued orders in 2014 that could shut many of the island's landfills.125 For the longer term, Puerto Rico has been exploring the use of biofuels, primarily those derived from agricultural wastes,126 and technologies that could harvest energy from the waves, currents, and ocean depths surrounding the islands.127
