Northern Mariana Islands Territory Energy Profile



Northern Mariana Islands Quick Facts

  • The Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands (CNMI) meets nearly all of its energy demand by importing petroleum products, including 22 million to 24 million gallons of diesel fuel annually to run the islands' five electricity generating plants.
  • Diesel fuel surcharges on electricity in the CNMI have ranged from 24 cents to 48 cents per kilowatthour in recent years, but recent decreases in world petroleum prices have brought the surcharge down from 30 cents per kilowatthour in April 2014 to 12 cents in January 2016. The average residential electricity bill declined 52% in that period. 
  • The Commonwealth Utilities Corp., the CNMI's public utility, has initiated an integrated resource planning (IRP) process to evaluate its long-term alternatives to petroleum-fired electricity generation.  
  • Active volcanoes make the CNMI–particularly the islands of Pagan and Saipan–unique in Micronesia in having significant geothermal energy potential.
  • The CNMI's renewable portfolio standard requires 20% of net electricity sales to come from renewable energy resources by 2016 if cost-effective resources are available. So far, only small-scale wind and solar resources have been built, mostly at government and school facilities.

Analysis

Last Updated: May 19, 2016

Overview

The Northern Mariana Islands import petroleum to meet nearly all of their energy needs.

The Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands (CNMI) is a chain of 14 islands and a handful of islets in the Pacific Ocean, located three-fourths of the way from Hawaii to the Philippines. The islands are the tops of a subsea mountain range whose eastern boundary is the Mariana Trench, the deepest known place on earth. The islands stretch northward in an arc from Guam toward Japan.1 The Northern Marianas have a tropical marine climate with little seasonal temperature variation, a dry season from December to June, and a rainy season from July to November. Typhoons can occur in the rainy season.2,3,4 The islands are typically thickly forested and have groundwater supplies.5 Coral reefs edge some of their shorelines, and two islands have active volcanoes. The territory has a total area of 179 square miles, about three times the size of Washington, DC.6,7

Like other U.S. Pacific islands, the CNMI has no conventional energy resources and meets nearly all of its energy needs with petroleum shipped in by tanker.8 Virtually all the population and all economic activity occur on three islands at the southern end of the island chain, near Guam: Saipan, Tinian, and Rota.9 About 9 in 10 residents live on Saipan, the largest island. The population totaled about 54,000 in the 2010 U.S. Census, but it has been shrinking.10,11,12,13 The shrinkage is the result of a shift in the CNMI's economy, as garment factories, once the largest industry, have closed and as foreign contract workers have left.14,15 The territorial government is now the largest contributor to the gross domestic product. Tourism, led by vacationers from Japan, is the largest private industry, accounting for about one-fourth of the CNMI's gross domestic product. Other industries include cattle ranching and small farming.16,17,18 The U.S. military's plans to expand bases on neighboring Guam may include using portions of Tinian and Pagan islands for military maneuvers.19

Petroleum

The CNMI neither produces20 nor refines petroleum.21 Petroleum products are imported by ship through harbors on Saipan, Tinian, and Rota.22 The three islands typically import about 550,000 barrels of diesel fuel annually for electricity generation alone, and the government sector typically consumes more than 800,000 barrels of diesel and 100,000 barrels of motor gasoline for transportation.23 U.S. law allows use of less expensive, high-sulfur motor gasoline in the Pacific territories.24 Other petroleum imports include liquefied petroleum gas, used mainly by businesses and households for cooking and heating water.25,26

Natural gas

The CNMI does not produce27 or consume natural gas.28

Coal

The CNMI does not produce29 or consume coal.30

Electricity

The commercial sector, led by tourism, is the largest electricity consumer in the Northern Mariana Islands.

The CNMI's electric system is owned and operated by the Commonwealth Utilities Corporation (CUC), a public corporation of the CNMI government.31 The CUC is regulated by the Commonwealth Public Utilities Commission.32 The CNMI has three small electric grids, one on each of the populated islands. Electricity is generated by five diesel-fueled power plants: three on Saipan and one each on Tinian and Rota. Nominal generating capability is about 70 megawatts on Saipan, 20 megawatts on Tinian, and 4.5 megawatts on Rota,33,34,35 but, because the generators are aging, full capability is not available. On Saipan, only about 57 megawatts were available36 before August 2015, when Saipan's power generation and distribution system was badly damaged by Typhoon Soudelor,37 leading to several months of power outages and disruptions of the public water supply and wastewater treatment systems.38,39,40,41

On Saipan, the commercial sector, including tourist facilities, consumes more than one-third of all power and is the largest consumer. Hotels use electricity mainly for air conditioning, water heating, and water purification. The residential sector is the second-largest consumer, using about one-fourth of all power.42 The CUC also operates street lighting, drinking water, and wastewater disposal systems,43 which together consume about one-twelfth of the electricity generated on Saipan.44 On Tinian, about half of the electricity is usually consumed by a hotel-casino complex and the federal government's International Broadcasting Bureau relay station. The hotel complex has recently suspended operations,45,46,47,48 but new resorts are in development.49

Electricity customers pay a fuel surcharge that varies with the world price of diesel fuel, which, in recent years, has brought the price of power to between three and four times the average in the 50 U.S. states.50,51 Most large hotels have generators and make electricity for their own use when grid supply is unreliable or fuel surcharges are high.52 With the declining world price for petroleum products, the CUC's fuel surcharge dropped from 30 cents per kilowatthour in April 2014 to 12 cents in January 2016, cutting the average residential power bill by 52%.53 Given the historic volatility of petroleum prices, the CUC has sought both conventional and renewable alternative electricity sources to diversify its diesel-dependent system,54 but the cost of new generation is a hurdle.55 The utility has been working on an integrated resource plan (IRP) to help evaluate its options,56,57 particularly in light of new tourist facilities proposed for development on the islands.58

Renewable energy

The CNMI government enacted a renewable portfolio standard (RPS) in 2006 that required the CUC to obtain 10% of its electricity from renewable energy sources in 2008, rising to 80% in 2014. The standard was later amended to require 20% renewable electricity by 2016.59 However, compliance is required only if there is a cost-effective way to meet the standard, and progress toward the standard has been limited.60,61 The CUC has solicited private project proposals for renewable energy technologies, most recently as part of its integrated resource planning (IRP) effort, but no utility-scale facilities have yet been built.62,63,64,65,66

Solar panels and small wind turbines are providing power for schools and government buildings.

Saipan, Tinian, and Rota are believed to have prevailing wind resources suitable for commercial turbines, but potential sites are limited because the islands are mountainous, land is scarce, and turbines may interfere with airstrip and military facilities. There are also concerns about turbine impacts on several threatened bird species.67,68 Turbines must also be designed to withstand typhoons.69

Solar energy resources are believed to be suitable for both solar hot water heating and for electricity generation by photovoltaic (PV) panels.70 CNMI law allows for net metering,71 and some rooftop solar PV projects are connected to the grid.72 Projects with combinations of solar panels and small wind turbines have been installed at schools and at other government buildings across the three islands to provide both power to the buildings and opportunities for students to learn about renewable energy.73,74 The projects include 250 solar panels with a combined generating capacity of 74.5 kilowatts and 16 wind turbines with a total capacity of 144 kilowatts.75,76 The CUC has also installed high-efficiency bulbs in its street lighting system.77 Because of the small size of the three islands' grids, variable wind and solar projects must be integrated carefully, possibly by combining advanced energy storage with renewable facilities.78

Geothermal technologies offer energy potential as the islands are part of known undersea volcanic formations. Several of the uninhabited northern islands have recently active volcanoes.79,80 Other renewable technologies that have been considered for the Northern Mariana Islands include conversion of non-recyclable municipal wastes to energy.81,82
