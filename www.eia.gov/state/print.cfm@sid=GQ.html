Guam Territory Energy Profile



Guam Quick Facts

  • Guam has no conventional energy resources and relies on petroleum products shipped in by tanker to meet almost all its energy needs.
  • As of late 2015, Guam Power Authority's surcharge for generator fuels was about 10 cents per kilowatthour, reflecting the cost of imported petroleum fuels.
  • Guam's first commercial solar farm, the 25.6-megawatt Dandan facility, was completed in October 2015, and the U.S. Navy has agreed to lease sites where 40 more solar megawatts could be installed.
  • In August 2015, an explosion and fire destroyed two of the Guam Power Authority's (GPA) primary baseload generators. Until the lost capacity can be repaired or replaced,  the GPA is leasing high-efficiency diesel generators.
  • Guam allows net metering, and an increasing number of customers are installing solar photovoltaic panels. As of early 2016, nearly 9 megawatts of distributed solar power was connected to Guam's grid.

Analysis

Last Updated: May 19, 2016

Overview

Guam imports petroleum products to meet almost all of its energy needs, including electricity generation.

Guam, the largest island in Micronesia, is located in the Pacific Ocean about three-fourths of the way from Hawaii to the Philippines. Surrounded by coral reef, Guam sits on the edge of the Mariana Trench and its Challenger Deep, the deepest known place on earth. Guam, like the neighboring Mariana Islands, is the top of an undersea mountain, part of a volcanic subsea range stretching northwest toward Japan. At 30 miles long and 4 to 12 miles wide, Guam has nearly three-and-a-half times the land area of Washington, DC.1,2,3 Guam has a tropical marine climate that is warm and humid with little seasonal temperature variation. The dry season runs from December to June, and the rainy season runs from July to November. The wet season can bring destructive typhoons.4,5

Guam has no fossil energy resources6 and meets nearly all of its energy needs, including electricity, with petroleum products7,8 shipped in by tanker.9 Guam's population is estimated to be about 162,000,10 plus 12,000 to 14,000 military personnel and their dependents.11 Tourism and the U.S. military are the two pillars of Guam's economy.12 Pending military plans to move some personnel from Okinawa, Japan, to Joint Region Marianas on Guam could bring a substantial influx of people to the island.13,14,15,16 The military already accounts for more than one-fifth of Guam's energy consumption.17 Per capita energy consumption on Guam is about two-thirds the average in the 50 states,18,19 and the island's energy intensityâ€”the amount of energy consumed per dollar of gross domestic productâ€”is typically slightly higher than the average for the states.20

Petroleum

Guam has set a goal of cutting petroleum consumption 20% from a 2010 baseline by 2020.

Guam has no petroleum production21 or refineries.22 All petroleum products are shipped in through its only port, located at Apra. Most petroleum products are imported from Japan.23 About half of all petroleum consumed is residual fuel oil, used to generate electricity. Motor gasoline and jet fuel each make up about one-fifth of total consumption. Diesel fuel and propane make up the balance of petroleum consumed.24 Guam's petroleum consumption per capita varies by year, but recently it has averaged nearly twice the consumption per capita in the 50 states.25,26 In 2012, the Guam government set a goal of cutting petroleum consumption 20% from a 2010 baseline by 2020.27 Since nearly one-third of petroleum use on-island (that is, not including aviation or shipping) in 2010 was in ground transportation, the government is aiming to increase the efficiency of vehicles on the island, improve traffic flows, reduce vehicle miles traveled, and increase use of biodiesel.28 The recent high price of motor gasoline has contributed to declining gasoline consumption.29

Natural gas

Guam does not produce30 or consume natural gas.31 As part of its integrated resource planning, the Guam Power Authority (GPA) has proposed replacing or converting some older generators to burn liquefied natural gas (LNG) instead of petroleum.32,33 The conversion would diversify GPA's energy sources and enable utility compliance with stricter environmental requirements.34 However, the GPA's regulator, the Guam Public Utilities Commission (PUC), has questioned the cost of infrastructure, such as storage, pipelines, and tanker terminals, needed to supply LNG to GPA generating plants.35

Coal

Guam does not produce36 or consume coal.37

Electricity

Electricity is provided by the GPA, a public corporation overseen by the elected Consolidated Commission on Utilities (CCU) and regulated by the Guam PUC. GPA owns and manages the island's power grid.38 GPA's electricity is generated primarily from No. 6 residual fuel oil, with a small amount from No. 2 distillate. Some hotels also have their own generators to produce power and hot water.39,40

In the 1990s, facing the need for major reinvestment in its baseload electricity generating facilities, GPA began contracting with independent power providers (IPPs) to upgrade and operate the island's baseload power generators. All GPA baseload generating facilities are now managed by IPPs under long-term contracts.41 Until an August 2015 explosion, the GPA's nominal generating capacity was 525 megawatts, nearly twice its historic peak load of 280 megawatts, giving the island grid backup capacity.42

An August 2015 explosion destroyed two of the Guam Power Authority’s primary baseload generators, forcing localized power rationing.

Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) rules require substantial modifications to reduce emissions at those generating plants.43 In late 2014, GPA received CCU approval for a plan to replace GPA's baseload petroleum-fired capacity with a single new generating plant that could burn both LNG and clean diesel.44,45 However, the project had not gone forward when, in August 2015, an explosion and fire destroyed two of GPA's newer baseload generators46 at the Cabras station. The GPA lost about one-seventh of its nominal generating capacity, leaving the island with periodic power rationing and localized power outages. The GPA has asked large hotels, shopping malls, and military facilities to use their own generators when possible.47,48 Until the GPA and the PUC agree on how to replace the lost capacity, the GPA is leasing high-efficiency diesel generators to stabilize its electricity supply.49,50

Electricity costs on Guam in recent years have run two to three times higher than in the 50 states, although costs are still less than on many Pacific islands.51,52,53 Even with declining world oil prices, the most recent surcharge for fuel costs was about 10 cents per kilowatthour.54 The high cost of petroleum products since 2008 has spurred electricity conservation among Guam consumers, producing a downward trend in consumption.55,56 The commercial sector is the largest electricity consumer, using more than one-third of all power. That sector includes hotels and restaurants serving tourists. The residential sector accounts for three-tenths of electricity use, and the U.S. military, one-fifth.57 Faced with the volatile cost of importing petroleum fuels, the GPA is funding energy efficiency management programs and installing smart meters to improve system operations.58,59,60 Guam also offers net metering, and a small but increasing number of customers have begun installing their own distributed generation.61,62

Renewable energy

A prime goal of Guam's economic development strategy is to substitute sustainable local energy resources for imported petroleum.63,64 Guam's energy goal is to reduce fossil fuel consumption in all sectors 20% from 2010 levels by 2020.65 Multiple opportunities for energy efficiency and renewable energy applications are being considered.66 In 2008, Guam's legislature enacted a renewable portfolio goal of having renewable sources provide 5% of net electricity sales by 2015 and 25% of sales by 2035. Under the goal, any new baseload electrical generating plant must obtain 10% of its total generating capacity from alternative energy sources. Solar photovoltaic (PV), wind, biomass, wave energy, and ocean thermal energy are all recognized as renewable sources.67 Since 2000, Guam's building code has required new homes to be configured so that solar hot water heating can be installed.68 Military bases have added solar PV arrays and solar water heaters to their living quarters.69,70

Until recently, little renewable energy was available on the island beyond a few solar PV units used for cell phone towers and remote weather stations, solar thermal units used for water heating, and a few small wind generators (less than 5 kilowatts capacity) operated by commercial and residential users.71,72 The Guam Power Authority released its Phase 1 renewables solicitation in 2011, and concluded contracts for 35 megawatts of solar and wind power.73 In 2015, Guam's first commercial solar PV facility, NRG Energy's 26-megawatt Dandan solar farm, began operation,74 but the planned Phase 1 wind facility has been delayed.75 In early 2016, the GPA inaugurated a pilot wind project, a single 275-kilowatt turbine at Cotal.76 The U.S. Navy has agreed to lease several sites to the GPA for solar facilities,77 and bids have been received for additional commercial projects under the GPA's Phase 2 renewables plan.78 However, in spring 2016, the GPA recommended scrapping the Phase 2 bids and restructuring Phase 2 to require battery storage systems with all projects. GPA officials said experience with the first solar farm, along with 9 megawatts of net metered solar energy now on Guam's small grid, showed storage is a necessity for grid stability.79

Wind turbines require special engineering to cope with Guam’s earthquakes and typhoons.

Guam has substantial wind potential but also unique siting issues. The island is seismically active and is in the Pacific's Typhoon Alley, so wind turbines must be engineered to resist both earthquakes and typhoon-force winds. Wind turbine siting must also consider impacts on military facilities, endangered species, and other local environmental concerns. Another challenge is maintaining stability of the island's small electric grid given the variability of wind power.80 However, wind power remains a long-term option in Guam energy planning.81,82

Guam has limited known geothermal resources, but geothermal is considered a potential future resource.83 Two ocean-based technologies being investigated are Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion (creating electricity from the temperature differences between ocean layers) and Sea Water Air Conditioning (using cold ocean water for chillers). Both technologies would involve pipes to access cold water deep in the ocean, and applications may be limited by pipe impacts on the fragile coral reef surrounding Guam.84,85
