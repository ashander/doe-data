Profile AnalysisPrint State Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: April 21, 2016

Overview

Bordered on the north by the Ohio River, Kentucky stretches from the Appalachian Mountains in the east to the flat plain of the Mississippi River in the west.1 In between, the rolling hills of the state's fertile Bluegrass region extend southward from the Ohio River to the Pennyroyal region, which is famous for its thousands of caverns and springs, including Mammoth Cave National Park.2 Major coal deposits are found both in the Central Appalachian Basin in the eastern part of the state and in the Illinois Basin in the northwest.3,4 Dams on the Tennessee, Cumberland, Ohio, and Laurel Rivers provide Kentucky with hydroelectric power.5 The state also has some oil and natural gas resources.6,7 Although two-thirds of Kentucky's agricultural economy is livestock, primarily thoroughbred horses and beef cattle,8 the state's abundant rain, temperate climate, and fertile soils9 provide ideal conditions for several crops.10 Kentucky's most important crops are tobacco, soybeans, corn, and wheat.11 Corn and beverage waste from distilleries in the state provide feedstock for Kentucky's ethanol production.12

Kentucky is among the top 10 states in energy use per dollar of gross domestic product.13 The industrial sector leads the state's energy demand.14 Low energy prices have helped attract manufacturing to Kentucky,15 and the state's largest manufacturing industries include motor vehicles, food, beverage and tobacco products, and chemicals. The state is also a major transportation and logistics hub.16 Kentucky is a net energy supplier to the nation, largely because of its coal production.17

Petroleum

Kentucky's crude oil production has averaged fewer than 10,000 barrels per day for almost two decades,18 and the state accounts for only about 0.1% of U.S. crude oil production.19 The oil produced in the state comes primarily from fields in western and south-central Kentucky.20,21 The state's annual crude oil production of about 3 million barrels22 is less than 3% of Kentucky demand.23 Crude oil and petroleum products enter the state via several pipelines.24

Kentucky has two oil refineries that can process nearly 246,000 barrels of oil per day.

Kentucky has two oil refineries, located in the cities of Catlettsburg and Somerset. The Catlettsburg refinery in the state's northeast can process about 240,000 barrels of crude oil per calendar day25 and produces motor gasoline, distillates, asphalt, heavy fuel oil, propane, and other petroleum products.26 The Somerset refinery in southeastern Kentucky can process about 5,500 barrels of crude oil per calendar day into motor gasoline, kerosene, diesel fuel, and heating oil that is marketed in the region.27

The transportation sector consumes nearly three-fourths of all petroleum products in Kentucky.28 Motor gasoline makes up the largest share of the petroleum consumed in Kentucky.29 Although most of the state can use conventional gasoline, the counties around Louisville, Kentucky, and to the south of Cincinnati, Ohio, are voluntary opt-in areas for the use of gasoline reformulated with ethanol to reduce air pollution.30,31

Natural gas

Kentucky's annual natural gas production rose from about 90 billion cubic feet in the mid-2000s and peaked at slightly more than 135 billion cubic feet in 2010,32 reaching 0.5% of total U.S. natural gas production. Natural gas prices subsequently declined, and Kentucky production fell back to less than 80 billion cubic feet by 2014.33,34 Most of the state's natural gas is produced from wells in eastern Kentucky,35,36 and the state's natural gas resources in the Devonian Shale underlying two-thirds of the state may exceed 100 trillion cubic feet.37

Natural gas production in Kentucky is lower than consumption,38 and several major interstate natural gas pipelines supply Kentucky consumers.39 Most natural gas entering Kentucky originates in the Gulf region and comes by pipeline through Tennessee, but, in 2014, Kentucky also began receiving natural gas from the Marcellus and Utica shales by pipeline from Ohio and West Virginia.40 More than nine-tenths of the natural gas entering Kentucky is shipped on to other states, primarily Indiana, Illinois, and Tennessee. Kentucky has 23 underground natural gas storage facilities that comprise 2.5% of U.S. storage capacity.41,42

Kentucky's industrial sector uses nearly one-half of the natural gas consumed in the state. The electric power sector doubled its natural gas consumption from 2014 to 2015 and is now the second-largest consuming sector. The residential sector receives about one-fifth of the natural gas delivered to end users,43 and 4 out of 10 households in Kentucky use natural gas as their primary fuel for home heating.44 Natural gas use per capita by Kentucky's residential sector is below the national average.45,46

Coal

Kentucky accounts for almost one-fifth of U.S. coal production east of the Mississippi River.

For decades, Kentucky has been the third-largest coal-producing state, after Wyoming and West Virginia.47,48 Kentucky typically accounts for almost 8% of total U.S. coal production and nearly one-fifth of production east of the Mississippi River.49 However, coal production in Kentucky is declining as coal-fired electricity generating plants that have been customers of Kentucky mines are being retired. Preliminary state figures put 2015 coal production at 61.4 million short tons, the lowest level since 1954.50,51 All of Kentucky's coal is bituminous.52 About one-fourth of all U.S. coal mines are located in Kentucky, more coal mines than in any other state. Although nearly two-thirds of those are surface mines, the state's underground mines produce seven-tenths of the coal mined in Kentucky.53 Kentucky's estimated total recoverable coal reserves are the fifth largest in the nation.54

The Central Appalachian (CAPP) coal delivery zone, an area around where the Big Sandy River flows into the Ohio River, is where benchmark prices for eastern U.S. coal are determined. Kentucky, Ohio, and West Virginia meet at the rivers' confluence, and bituminous coal arrives from mines in those states, as well as in Tennessee and Virginia, typically by rail or truck. The coal is delivered for shipment by multi-car trains and barges to final customers, including coal-fired electricity generating plants, industrial plants, commercial and institutional facilities, coke plants, and shipping ports.55

Kentucky ships more than two-thirds of its coal production out of state. Coal is sent to more than 20 other states, where it is primarily used by power plants to generate electricity.56 Kentucky also usually exports about one-tenth of the coal it produces.57,58

Electricity

In 2015, for the first time in decades, coal generated less than nine-tenths of Kentucky's net electricity.

Coal-fired power plants have routinely produced more than nine-tenths of Kentucky's net electricity generation,59 but, in 2015, coal accounted for less than nine-tenths of net generation for the first time in decades. Natural gas-fired electricity production more than doubled, to 7% of net generation. However, Kentucky still relied on coal to generate a greater share of its electricity than any states except West Virginia and Wyoming.60 The rest of Kentucky's electricity generation comes from hydroelectric power plants, with a small contribution from biomass.61

As of 2014, Kentucky had the fourth-largest amount of coal-fired generating capacity in the nation, after Texas, Indiana, and Ohio.62 But with coal-fired generating units aging and becoming more costly to operate, a number of coal-fired power plants in Kentucky are being shut down or retrofitted to burn natural gas.63,64 In 2015 alone, 10% of Kentucky's coal-fired electricity generating capacity was shut down.65 A total of nearly 3,000 megawatts of coal-fired generating capacity in the state has been retired or is scheduled to be retired between 2012 and 2025.66 At Kentucky's largest power plant, the 2,550-megawatt coal-fired Paradise Plant,67 the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA) is replacing units 1 and 2, which have a combined capacity of more than 1,400 megawatts, with natural gas-fired capacity by 2017.68,69

Kentucky has not restructured its electric utility industry. Utilities in the state remain vertically integrated generators, transmitters, and distributors of electric power.70 Electricity is supplied to consumers by three investor-owned electric utilities, 26 cooperatives, 20 municipal utilities, and the TVA.71 Electricity rates vary by provider, but, on average in 2015, Kentucky had the fifth lowest rates of any state, and the lowest rates east of the Mississippi River.72,73 About half of Kentucky households use electricity as their primary heating source.74

Renewable energy

Renewable resources are a small part of Kentucky's energy mix, and the state has no renewable energy standard or goals.75 The largest source of renewable energy in Kentucky is hydroelectric power, which provides about 4% of the state's electricity generation76 at 6 dams around the state.77 Biomass accounts for the other 0.5% of the state's net generation from renewables.78 Wood wastes are the largest source of biomass for electricity.79

Kentucky has few wind resources suitable for developing utility-scale power projects80 and no commercial wind power facilities, but the state does have some solar potential.81 Kentucky's first utility-scale solar photovoltaic (PV) generating facility, the 2-megawatt Bowling Green Solar Farm, came online in 2011 and sells its power to the TVA.82 A second utility-scale solar farm, a 10-megawatt facility located at the E.W. Brown coal-fired generating station near Harrodsburg, Kentucky, began construction in 2016.83 Increasing amounts of distributed solar PV capacity, from home rooftops to industrial systems, have been installed across the state.84 Solar facilities are by far the largest component of distributed generation in Kentucky, with more than 12 megawatts of solar PV installed.85 In 2015, three-fourths of distributed solar generation in Kentucky came from commercial-sector facilities.86 Kentucky is home to the nation's first net-zero energy-use public school building. The school design combines energy efficient systems, geothermal heat pumps, daylight harvesting, and a thin film rooftop solar PV system.87 Kentucky law provides for net metering of distributed generation from solar, wind, hydro, biomass, and biogas facilities of 30 kilowatts or less. Each power provider's obligation to connect eligible customer generators is limited to 1% of the provider's peak load in the previous year.88

Kentucky has two ethanol plants with a combined capacity of about 35 million gallons per year.89 Most of the ethanol is produced at a plant owned by a farmers' cooperative that uses corn as its primary feedstock.90,91 The smaller ethanol facility is a recycling operation in an abandoned bourbon distillery that produces ethanol from waste non-alcoholic and alcoholic beverages, sugars, industrial alcohols, health and beauty alcohols, and pharmaceutical manufacturing by-products.92 Kentucky also has three biodiesel production plants with a combined capacity of about 47 million gallons per year. The biodiesel plants use multiple feedstocks, including waste vegetable oil and soy oil.93
