Profile AnalysisPrint Territory Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: May 19, 2016

Overview

American Samoa, the southernmost territory of the United States, is part of a tropical island chain located about halfway between Hawaii and New Zealand. It consists of the adjacent islands of Tutuila and Aunu'u; the Manu'a group of Ta'u, Ofu, and Olosega; and two coral atolls, Swains and Rose islands. The total land area, 76 square miles, is slightly larger than that of Washington, DC.1,2 Following Polynesian tradition, most land is communally owned by extended families.3

American Samoa depends almost entirely on petroleum imports for its energy needs.

The volcanic Samoan Islands chain, which includes both American Samoa and the independent nation of Samoa, lies at the northern end of the Tonga Trench, a highly active seismic zone.4,5 The climate is tropical marine, with little seasonal temperature variation. A rainy season runs from November to April,6 but rain falls throughout the year.7 The annual average rainfall is about 125 inches at sea level and up to 300 inches in the mountains.8 American Samoa is home to about 57,000 people.9,10 Most of the population lives on the largest island, Tutuila, where the deepwater port of Pago Pago is located.11 American Samoa's largest industry is tuna canning, which accounts for most of the territory's exports.12 American Samoa's tuna industry shrank when one of two canneries shut down in 2009,13,14 but that cannery reopened under a new owner in 2015.15 The territory's other major economic sectors are government and agriculture.16 Government agencies employ more than one-fourth of all workers. American Samoa's potential for tourism is limited by its distance from population centers. The territory's gross domestic product (GDP) per capita is one-sixth of the GDP per capita in the 50 U.S. states.17

The territory lacks fossil energy resources and depends on imported petroleum products to meet most energy needs. Volatile petroleum product prices are a major concern for the islands' economy,18 which typically has been more than twice as energy intensive as that of the 50 U.S. states,19 although per capita energy consumption has been about one-half of the average for the states.20,21 Energy consumption dropped sharply after 2009, when an earthquake and tsunami devastated Tutuila just as one cannery was closing, putting one in five island employees out of work. The economy has been slowly recovering, aided in part by infusions of federal economic and disaster recovery assistance.22,23,24

Petroleum

American Samoa does not produce25 or refine petroleum.26 Petroleum products are imported in tankers, which unload at a terminal and tank farm adjacent to the main harbor at Pago Pago. The territory imports distillates, mainly low-sulfur diesel fuel, high-sulfur marine fuel, jet fuel, and motor gasoline.27 Before the 2009 tsunami, American Samoans typically consumed about one-third more petroleum per capita than the average for the U.S. states. By 2011, island residents' consumption fell to about 80% of the average per capita consumption for the states.28,29

Natural gas

American Samoa does not produce30 or consume natural gas.31

Coal

American Samoa does not produce32 or consume coal.33

Electricity

American Samoans use one-fourth as much electricity per capita as U.S. states consumers but may pay five times as much per kilowatthour.

Nearly all of American Samoa's electricity is supplied by generators consuming No. 2 diesel fuel. The American Samoa Power Authority (ASPA), a government corporation, owns and operates two generating plants and the electric grid on Tutuila plus two other small generating plants and grids serving the Manu'a group. Total generating capacity is about 40 megawatts, most of it from the Tafuna and Satala plants on Tutuila.34 ASPA also provides drinking water and wastewater treatment.35 Pumping, treating, distributing, and collecting water require a significant share of ASPA's electricity.36 The September 2009 earthquake and tsunami destroyed the Satala generating plant, reducing generating capacity on Tutuila by half.37 Leased generators burning ultra-low-sulfur diesel temporarily replaced those destroyed in 2009, which had used high-sulfur diesel fuel.38 A 24.5-megawatt replacement Satala plant, with diesel generating units that are 25% more efficient than Satala's old units, is scheduled for completion in 2016.39 The plant's foundation has been re-engineered to better resist earthquakes.40

The residential sector is the largest electricity consumer, using nearly one-third of all power generated on American Samoa. The commercial sector uses almost as much power as the residential sector. The government consumes nearly one-fifth of electricity generated on the islands.41 Per capita consumption is about one-fourth of per capita consumption in the 50 U.S. states.42,43,44,45 Electricity cost varies with a fuel surcharge linked to world oil prices.46 In early 2012, that surcharge brought the average electricity price in American Samoa to nearly five times the average price in the 50 states.47,48 By 2016, with the decline in world petroleum prices, the monthly residential rate was about 2.5 times the average in the states, comparable to the rate in Hawaii.49

Renewable energy

With American Samoa's high cost of electricity and geographic isolation, the government established a Renewable Energy Committee to work with federal experts to bring sustainable renewable energy to the islands.50,51 The committee has developed energy strategies to take advantage of solar, wind, and geothermal potential on Tutuila52,53 and has launched a project to supply the tiny Manu'a islands' grids completely with renewables.54 The government also offers assistance for residential weatherization.55

American Samoa obtains 2.4% of its electricity from solar power and has substantial potential to expand solar energy use.

In 2008, American Samoa adopted a net metering law that allowed owners of small solar or wind facilities, installed primarily for the consumer's use, to receive credit for excess power sent to the grid.56 As of late 2015, ASPA obtained about 2.4% of its electricity from solar photovoltaic (PV) facilities. ASPA's largest solar resource is a 1.75-megawatt solar PV complex near the Tafuna power station,57 and customers have installed more than 700 kilowatts of solar PV.58 Customer installations include 41 arrays on rooftops of government and private buildings, and solar hot water heating for Tutuila's Lyndon B. Johnson Tropical Medical Center.59 Another 1.2 megawatts of utility solar PV capacity is being installed at two sites.60 Samoa is also developing hybrid solar and energy storage systems to supply the Manu'a islands' grids, with an eventual goal of 100% renewable electricity there. The Ofu-Olosega system, which serves about 350 people, will have 341 kilowatts of solar PV paired with 1 megawatthour of storage capability, while the Ta'u system, serving 790 people, will have 1,200 kilowatts of solar PV and 3 megawatthours of storage. The hybrid system will supply 75% of Ofu-Olosega demand and 80% of Ta'u demand. ASPA is considering adding an in-stream hydropower turbine on the Ofu-Olosega system to reach the goal of 100% renewables.61,62,63 American Samoa is close to the equator, so it has substantial potential to expand use of solar energy.64

ASPA is installing Organic Rankine Cycle technology to capture waste heat emitted by diesel generators at its Tafuna plant to produce additional electricity.65,66 The utility has also signed an agreement to buy electricity generated at a waste-to-energy plant to be built at Tutuila's Futiga landfill, which could produce up to 10% of ASPA's electricity needs.67 The plant could also help reduce waste volumes at the landfill, which is nearing capacity.68

An ASPA study has identified some potential wind power sites around Tutuila,69 and two 100-kilowatt turbines are being installed.70 Challenges for wind energy include typhoons, social acceptance, and grid stability.71 American Samoa's communal land ownership structure also makes long-term leasing for larger scale projects a potential hurdle for development.72 Geothermal energy is being actively explored as well, since preliminary studies of the island's geology show some promise and indicate geothermal energy could be economical for the islands.73,74
