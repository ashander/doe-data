Profile AnalysisPrint State Energy Profile
(overview, data, & analysis)

Last Updated: February 18, 2016

Overview

South Dakota's vast prairie stretches from the lowlands in the east to the Black Hills in the west.1 Nine-tenths of the state's varied landscape is covered by crops or pastureland.2 Modest crude oil and natural gas production is concentrated in western South Dakota, and substantial renewable resources are found statewide.3 High winds that blow unobstructed across the state's prairie provide a significant wind energy resource.4,5 Solar resources are greatest in southwestern South Dakota and geothermal potential is present across much of the state.6,7 The Missouri River, the largest of many rivers in the state, cuts across the prairie, contributing to the state's hydroelectric resources.8 Along the river's course through South Dakota are several large hydroelectric dams.9

Agriculture is the leading industry in South Dakota, a state with more livestock than people.10,11,12 One-third of South Dakota's agricultural economy comes from the production of beef cattle, and the state is one of the nation's top 10 corn producers.13 The abundant corn crop is used, in part, to fuel South Dakota's ethanol production, and the state ranks among the six largest ethanol producers in the country.14

South Dakota ranks among the six largest ethanol producing states in the nation.

Industry is South Dakota's leading energy-consuming sector, with the transportation sector a distant second.15 The industrial sector includes South Dakota's many farms, as well as its growing manufacturing industries. Food processing and the manufacture of farm and construction machinery, fabricated metal products, transportation equipment, and computers are the state's leading manufacturing activities.16 South Dakota has one of the smallest populations of any state, and its total energy consumption is among the lowest in the nation. However, with a climate that is typified by hot summers, exceptionally cold winters, and periodic droughts, South Dakota is one of the top 10 states in total energy consumption per capita.17,18,19

Petroleum

South Dakota's crude oil production is less than 0.1% of the nation's total.20 Petroleum production is concentrated in the western corner of the state, where Harding County produces the bulk of South Dakota's crude oil and natural gas.21 Although the Williston Basin extends into South Dakota from the north, the productive Bakken Shale does not.22 Oil production in South Dakota has been fairly steady for decades, ranging between about 1.1 million and 1.8 million barrels per year.23 South Dakota does not have significant proved petroleum reserves, and it does not have any oil refineries.24,25

South Dakota's ethanol plants have an operating capacity of more than 1 billion gallons per year.

South Dakota has one major crude oil pipeline that crosses the state. Several petroleum product pipelines that enter South Dakota from neighboring states supply refined products to South Dakota consumers.26 About one-sixth of South Dakota's households use liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) as their primary fuel for home heating. A total of about one-fifth of the state's homes use some sort of petroleum product, either LPG, fuel oil, or kerosene, for heating, but almost half of the petroleum consumed in South Dakota is in the form of motor gasoline.27,28 South Dakota allows the statewide use of conventional gasoline without ethanol.29,30 The operating capacity of South Dakota's ethanol plants is slightly more than 1 billion gallons per year, but only about 45 million gallons per year are consumed in the state.31

Natural gas

Like crude oil, natural gas production in South Dakota has been modest. In the past decade, overall gross withdrawals in the state have experienced an increase of about one-third, but South Dakota accounts for only about 0.05% of U.S. natural gas production.32 Most natural gas produced in the state comes from wells drilled into the Pierre Shale.33 Although South Dakota, with its small population, uses relatively little natural gas, more natural gas is consumed in the state than is produced.34,35 Industry, including agriculture, is the largest natural gas-consuming sector in South Dakota. The residential sector is the second-largest consumer in the state, but it uses only about one-third as much as the industrial sector.36 Nearly half of South Dakota households use natural gas as their primary fuel for home heating.37

Almost all of the natural gas that enters South Dakota comes from North Dakota. More than nine-tenths of the natural gas received in the state is shipped on to Minnesota and other states.38 South Dakota does not have any underground natural gas storage facilities.39

Coal

South Dakota does not have any coal production or reserves and relies on shipments of coal from other states to meet its limited coal demand.40,41 All of the coal brought into the state comes from Wyoming and Montana, and most of it is used for electricity generation.42,43 A small portion of the coal delivered to South Dakota is used at industrial facilities.44

Electricity

South Dakota uses a variety of sources for electricity generation, but hydroelectric power supplies almost half of the state's net generation.45 Several of the state's largest dams are operated by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. The hydroelectric power produced in South Dakota is marketed and delivered throughout the central and western United States by the Western Area Power Administration.46

Hydroelectric power supplies almost half of South Dakota's net generation.

The Missouri River cuts through the state, and the river basin has several dams. The Oahe Dam is the northernmost dam in South Dakota. Forming the fourth-largest man-made reservoir in the nation, the Oahe began generating hydroelectric power in 1962. The Big Bend Dam was built where a unique bend in the Missouri River in south-central South Dakota brings the river to a nearly complete loop. The Big Bend Dam's first generating unit went into operation in 1964. Fort Randall Dam, in South Dakota just north of the Nebraska state line, first produced power in March 1954. The Gavins Point Dam, completed in 1957, straddles the border between South Dakota and Nebraska and is the smallest of the Upper Missouri River Basin dams. Water released from upstream dams is stored at the Gavins Point Dam in Lewis and Clark Lake for production of hydroelectric power.47 South Dakota's remaining net electricity generation is supplied almost entirely from coal and wind. A small amount of South Dakota's electricity is generated from natural gas.48

Per capita retail electricity sales in South Dakota are above the national average, and nearly three-tenths of the state's households use electricity as their primary energy source for home heating.49,50,51 The residential and commercial sectors together account for almost four-fifths of retail electricity sales in the state; the industrial sector accounts for the rest.52

Renewable energy

South Dakota uses its renewable energy resources extensively. In addition to being among the nation's leading producers of ethanol, South Dakota generates about two-thirds to three-fourths of its electricity from hydroelectric power and wind, with more generation from hydroelectric power than from any other source.53,54

South Dakota has the sixth-largest wind resource in the nation.

South Dakota has the sixth largest wind resource in the nation.55 More than one-fourth of the state's net generation is from wind, and South Dakota ranks second in the nation after Iowa in the percentage of its net electricity generation provided by wind.56 Even with more than 500 wind turbines statewide, South Dakota's wind potential is just beginning to be developed.57 Almost nine-tenths of the state has been identified as suitable for utility-scale wind projects, and about 175 megawatts of utility-scale wind capacity was installed in 2015.58,59,60

South Dakota has other undeveloped energy resources. An area in the south-central part of the state has high-temperature geothermal potential.61 Additional lower-temperature geothermal resources in South Dakota have been used in direct heat applications, including providing heat for district heating, geothermal heat pumps, spas, swimming pools, and numerous ranches.62 South Dakota has no utility-scale electricity generation from biomass, but, as an agricultural state, it has abundant agricultural waste that could be processed into biofuels.63,64 Sixteen ethanol plants are in operation in South Dakota, and all of them use corn as a feedstock.65 The state has no utility-scale solar generation and only moderate solar photovoltaic potential across most of the state. The greatest solar potential is in the state's southwest corner.66,67

In February 2008, South Dakota's legislature established a renewable portfolio objective with the goal of obtaining 10% of all retail electricity sales from renewable and recycled energy sources by 2015. In 2009, the policy was amended to allow conserved energy as a component. The legislation applied to all retail providers of electricity in the state. Compliance with the renewable portfolio objective was voluntary.68 Most of the electricity providers in the state are expected to have met the goal.69 South Dakota has additional regulatory policies and financial incentives aimed at encouraging the expanded use of renewable sources for electricity generation in the state.70

Energy on Tribal Lands

More than 72,000 Native Americans live in South Dakota.71 Almost one-tenth of the state is land held by tribes or individual tribal members, giving South Dakota the fifth-largest amount of acreage in tribal hands in the nation.72 The state is home to nine federally recognized tribes on nine reservations. Two of the reservations straddle the North Dakota-South Dakota border. 73,74 The Pine Ridge, Cheyenne River, Rosebud, and Standing Rock Tribes are some of the largest tribal landholders in the United States.75

Although South Dakota's tribal lands do not hold significant fossil energy resources, they do have substantial renewable energy resources. The Sisseton Tribe of northeastern South Dakota is one of the five tribes in the nation with the greatest biomass potential on their reservations. The Pine Ridge reservation in southwestern South Dakota has some of the greatest concentrating solar power potential, and the Standing Rock reservation that straddles the North and South Dakota border is one of the five reservations with the greatest rural utility-scale solar photovoltaic potential.76 Several utility-scale wind projects are in development on South Dakota tribal lands.77 In 2003, South Dakota's Rosebud Sioux Tribe installed a 750-kilowatt wind turbine. It was the first tribal-owned and tribal-operated utility-scale commercial wind turbine in the Lower 48 states.78 In 2013, six South Dakota tribes announced the collaborative development of an interconnected grid of wind farms. The planned facilities could provide up to 2 gigawatts of capacity.79 To expedite development of the wind project, including transmission lines, a tribally owned Multi-Tribal Power Authority has been formed by eight Sioux tribes in South Dakota—the Cheyenne River Sioux, Crow Creek Sioux, Flandreau Santee Sioux, Oglala Sioux, Rosebud Sioux, Sisseton-Wahpeton Oyate, Standing Rock Sioux, and Yankton Sioux.80 In 2014, the South Dakota Crow Creek Sioux Tribe received a federal grant to begin work on a 100- to 400-megawatt wind farm project on 7,000 acres of its tribal land.81

Tribal leaders in South Dakota are exploring ways to lower their energy costs. South Dakota and neighboring states have abundant hydroelectric resources, much of it generated at federal dams.82 The power generated at these facilities is distributed to electric utilities by the Western Area Power Administration (WAPA). The tribes want to lower their energy costs by receiving hydroelectricity directly from the WAPA instead of from third-party utilities.83
